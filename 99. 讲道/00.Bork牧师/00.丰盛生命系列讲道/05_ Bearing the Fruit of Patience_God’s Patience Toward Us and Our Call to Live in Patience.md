# Sermon: Bearing the Fruit of Patience

**Theme: God’s Patience Toward Us, and Our Call to Live in Patience**

-----

## Introduction: God’s Patience Is Not to Be Abused, But Recognized

We should not hold God’s patience in contempt or abuse it, but rather recognize it as God’s way of drawing us closer to Him for the forgiveness we need. Paul’s words to the Roman Christians, in chapter two, speak to this:

> “You therefore have no excuse, you who pass judgment on someone else, for at whatever point you judge another, you are condemning yourself, because you who pass judgment do the same things. Now we know that God’s judgment against those who do such things is based on truth. So when you, a mere human being, pass judgment on them and yet do the same things, do you think you will escape God’s judgment? Or do you show contempt for the riches of his kindness, forbearance and patience, not realizing that God’s kindness is intended to lead you to repentance?” (Romans 2:1–4)

God’s Word encourages us to go to Him for the forgiveness we need, recognizing that we need Him, and how ready He is to give it.

Jesus illustrated this in one of His parables, often called the Parable of the Prodigal Son. In it we see the loving, patient forgiveness of the father — and that illustrates for us the loving patience of God toward us. Hear God’s Word as Luke records this parable of Jesus in chapter 15 of his Gospel:

> “Jesus continued: ‘There was a man who had two sons. The younger one said to his father, “Father, give me my share of the estate.” So he divided his property between them. Not long after that, the younger son got together all he had, set off for a distant country, and there squandered his wealth in wild living. After he had spent everything, there was a severe famine in that whole country, and he began to be in need. So he went and hired himself out to a citizen of that country, who sent him to his fields to feed pigs. He longed to fill his stomach with the pods that the pigs were eating, but no one gave him anything. When he came to his senses, he said, “How many of my father’s hired servants have food to spare, and here I am starving to death. I will set out and go back to my father and say to him, ‘Father, I have sinned against heaven and against you. I am no longer worthy to be called your son; make me like one of your hired servants.’” So he got up and went to his father. But while he was still a long way off, his father saw him and was filled with compassion for him; he ran to his son, threw his arms around him and kissed him. The son said to him, “Father, I have sinned against heaven and against you. I am no longer worthy to be called your son.” But the father said to his servants, “Quick! Bring the best robe and put it on him. Put a ring on his finger and sandals on his feet. Bring the fattened calf and kill it. Let’s have a feast and celebrate. For this son of mine was dead and is alive again; he was lost and is found.” So they began to celebrate. Meanwhile, the older son was in the field. When he came near the house, he heard music and dancing. So he called one of the servants and asked him, “What’s going on?” “Your brother has come,” he replied, “and your father has killed the fattened calf because he has him back safe and sound.” The older brother became angry and refused to go in. So his father went out and pleaded with him. But he answered his father, “Look! All these years I’ve been slaving for you and never disobeyed your orders. Yet you never gave me even a young goat so I could celebrate with my friends. But when this son of yours who has squandered your property with prostitutes comes home, you kill the fattened calf for him!” “My son,” the father said, “you are always with me, and everything I have is yours. But we had to celebrate and be glad, because this brother of yours was dead and is alive again; he was lost and is found.”’” (Luke 15:11–32)

Those words of Jesus remind us of the Father’s joy when we come back to Him in His patient love and forgiveness.

Now we’ll join to profess our faith this morning with a song we believe in.

## I. Clothed with Patience: A Fruit of Christian Character

Let me ask you a question: What does your wardrobe look like? I’m sure you have a variety of clothing, depending on the occasion — clothes required for work, maybe a uniform, or just something to look nice; clothes for a special occasion, when you dress up more formally; and, of course, the clothes we just like to relax in — even those grungy clothes we wear when doing yard work. A variety of clothes, depending on the situations or the people we’re with.

Why am I talking about clothes? Because the Apostle Paul used that as a metaphor — an illustration for us of what we are to “wear” with our faith. We might call it our core values, our Christian character. It fits in quite well with what we’re talking about this summer. Paul wrote:

> “Therefore, as God’s chosen people, holy and dearly loved, clothe yourselves with compassion, kindness, humility, gentleness and patience.” (Colossians 3:12)

When we look at that list, we see a number of things that also appear in another list Paul gives — the fruit of the Spirit: love, joy, peace, patience, and so on (Galatians 5:22–23). This summer, in a series of messages on living a fruitful life, we’re reminded that because of our connection with Jesus, we will bear much fruit. We’re taking a look at each fruit of the Spirit and considering how we bear it.

Today we’re looking at the fruit of patience — how to live a life of patience. But I have a guess that patience is probably the hardest fruit for us to grow.

What’s your patience like? If there were a way to measure it, would it come out high, medium, or pretty low?

Why is patience so hard for us? It’s because of our nature — we’re just thinking about ourselves and what we want. We’re impatient people; we want it right away, if not sooner. What leads to that is selfishness and greed — wanting things under our control, going our way, in our own time.

Some people have suggested there’s a way to develop patience — maybe through life experience: “I’ve been through this before, and I know that if you just give it time, it’ll all work out.” Or maybe through maturity: “We’ve been through these things before; we know what it takes to wait, or to work at it, so that it turns out fine.” But impatience can still sneak in, because of our sinful nature.

As with all the fruit of the Spirit, patience comes from God. Just as the fruit we eat starts with a seed, so the life of patience starts with the seed of God’s patience toward us. Let’s take a look at what Scripture teaches us about God’s patience with people.

-----

## II. God’s Patience: His Plan of Salvation

God declares that He has patience and shows it. The Apostle Peter points this out:

> “Bear in mind that our Lord’s patience means salvation, just as our dear brother Paul also wrote you with the wisdom that God gave him.” (2 Peter 3:15)

Paul wrote about it; God declares it in the Scriptures. He is patient because of our salvation.

The word “salvation” is kind of a churchy word, but it really means a *rescue*. When we think of rescue, we think of someone in a very dire situation — deadly, in fact — that they can’t get out of on their own. It requires fast action. Because we can’t get out of it on our own, we need action by God.

That situation with God came about because of our disobedience. We separated ourselves from God, and any right to His blessings and eternal life, because of our sin — our disobedience to all His laws.

But God had a rescue plan, one He developed even before the world was created: to send His own Son into this world to live that perfect, sinless life that you and I can’t live, and to give us that righteousness through faith in Him.

He then carried out the second part of that plan: to take the wrath of God — the justice of God against all our sins — upon Himself when He suffered on the cross at Calvary. To show that the plan worked, Jesus was raised from the dead, showing His power over death, and that our sins have been paid for, and that you and I will live eternally too.

That was God’s rescue plan — and yet we might say it took a long time. That was God’s patience at work then, and still at work today, even though we, as sinful people (the whole world sinful), continue to rebel against God. God is patient, again and again, to forgive us and bring us His salvation.

In fact, that’s salvation for the whole world. It isn’t just for a certain group of people — it’s for all. Jesus died for the sins of the whole world. That was God’s patience at work for everybody, that whoever would simply believe what Jesus has done would be saved.

-----

## III. Not Just Words, But Demonstrated Patience

Sometimes when we hear a sales pitch, we think: “Okay, those are nice-sounding words, but what’s the proof? Does it really work?” God doesn’t just talk about His patience — He demonstrated it for us.

Take a look at the history of the world recorded in Scripture. Go back and see God’s patience toward people.

**The people of Noah’s day.** God tells us in Scripture that when He looked out, He saw that every inclination of the human heart was evil from the very beginning. So what did He do? He demonstrated patience and gave the world 120 years to clean up their act and come back to Him.

Could you imagine a parent going into their kid’s room and saying, “This room is a disaster; you need to clean it up — but I’ll give you 120 years to do it, and when I come back, you better be clean”? God gave the world 120 years to come back to Him, but they didn’t — except for Noah and his family, who found grace in the eyes of God.

God sent the flood to wipe out the sinfulness of the world, but that was His grace at work — His patience — because by removing that unbelieving generation, He preserved Noah and his family to continue His patient plan to bring salvation, not just to Noah’s family, but to the rest of the world, because through Noah’s family came the Savior, Jesus.

God has all the plans worked out to save the world — all people, even the very worst of sinners — for Jesus died for everyone, including the worst of sinners.

**Paul’s testimony.** That’s how the Apostle Paul, the greatest evangelist, identified himself:

> “But for that very reason I was shown mercy so that in me, the worst of sinners, Christ Jesus might display his immense patience as an example for those who would believe in him and receive eternal life.” (1 Timothy 1:16)

Paul was once an unbeliever. He blasphemed Christ, saying He wasn’t God, wasn’t the Savior. He even tried to stamp out the message of Christianity by persecuting Christians, putting them in jail, overseeing their deaths. Paul called himself a murderer — but God showed patience to him. Through Jesus Christ, He displayed His immense patience as an example for those who would believe in Him and receive eternal life. That’s us.

When Paul calls himself the worst of sinners, I can’t help but think of some of the prisoners I’ve ministered to over the years — locked up, given long sentences for murder, drugs, stealing, abusing others. But when they were convicted, not only by the law of the land but by the law of God, they sought forgiveness and found grace in the eyes of God. I’ve had the chance to minister to them and share God’s salvation, forgiveness, and patience — and they came to believe.

Now, you and I, in our pride, probably wouldn’t include ourselves as being “the worst of sinners” — but we are too. We were born into this world without any faith in God. We were born with a sinful nature, as rebellious people who’ve sinned against God, breaking every one of His commandments. But God had grace toward us, bringing us to faith by hearing His Word, washing our sins away, and giving us faith in baptism. We’ve experienced the patient love of God in our lives, and therefore we appreciate it and hear God’s call to imitate it.

Peter talks about this:

> “The Lord is not slow in keeping his promise, as some understand slowness. Instead he is patient with you, not wanting anyone to perish, but everyone to come to repentance.” (2 Peter 3:9)

How do we come to that repentance? By knowing the grace of God to forgive. King Solomon writes this proverb:

> “A person’s wisdom yields patience.” (Proverbs 19:11, paraphrased)

The wisdom he was talking about, defined earlier in the book, is the fear of the Lord — having faith in God and His forgiveness. That’s what brings us to show patience in our life. Patience starts with the seed of God’s patience — seen, known, and trusted in His plan of salvation for us.

The writer to the Hebrews urges:

> “We do not want you to become lazy, but to imitate those who through faith and patience inherit what has been promised.” (Hebrews 6:12)

God calls us to be people of patience because of His patience with us. But patience is more than just waiting for it to happen — patience is waiting *with trust* in the grace and work of God.

-----

## IV. Do We Show Contempt for God’s Patience?

You and I have experienced the kindness and patience of God, but the question Paul asked in our first reading is: Do you disregard it? Do you devalue it? Do you not appreciate it, and live and follow it accordingly?

Paul said:

> “Do you show contempt for the riches of his kindness, forbearance and patience, not realizing that God’s kindness is intended to lead you to repentance?” (Romans 2:4)

Do you take God’s forgiveness for granted? Do you not appreciate it — not desire it, not want to thank and praise Him for it and grow your life in it? That’s what Paul is urging us to do.

The prophet Isaiah called out God’s people on this:

> “Hear now, you house of David! Is it not enough to try the patience of humans? Will you try the patience of my God also?” (Isaiah 7:13)

We try, or test, the patience of God by thinking, “I can go ahead and do this, because God will forgive it anyway,” or “I’m not going to think about the wrong of it — everybody does wrong.” But God calls us to repent — to acknowledge our sinfulness and receive that blessed assurance of forgiveness in Jesus.

Again, the writer to the Hebrews says:

> “See to it, brothers and sisters, that none of you has a sinful, unbelieving heart that turns away from the living God. But encourage one another daily, as long as it is called ‘today,’ so that none of you may be hardened by sin’s deceitfulness… Therefore, since the promise of entering his rest still stands, let us be careful that none of you be found to have fallen short of it.” (Hebrews 3:12–13; 4:1)

You and I have experienced the wonderful, loving patience of God. Let us stay faithful to Him. (More about that fruit of faithfulness will come in a few weeks; today, let’s just focus on patience, and respond to God’s patience toward us by putting patience into practice in our own lives.)

-----

## V. Living a Life of Patience

Paul starts by saying:

> “…being strengthened with all power according to his glorious might, so that you may have great endurance and patience.” (Colossians 1:11, paraphrased)

Here’s where patience comes from in our life — from God and His patience toward us. As said earlier, patience is not just waiting; it’s waiting with faith in God, trusting His work to bring us blessings. So look at the strength that comes from God — and you too can be patient.

**1. Patience in daily life — like the farmer**

James gives us a practical lesson:

> “Be patient, then, brothers and sisters, until the Lord’s coming. See how the farmer waits for the land to yield its valuable crop, patiently waiting for the autumn and spring rains. You too, be patient and stand firm, because the Lord’s coming is near.” (James 5:7–8)

James urges us to be patient throughout our life — whatever we work at, whatever responsibility we carry, whatever we’re waiting for — trusting God’s working, like the farmer who trusts God’s working through nature. Not only in the things we see and experience in our own lives, but throughout the universe, God brings us His blessings again and again. Count on that, just like the farmer counts on the rain to come. Count on God working in your life to bring blessing, looking forward to that final, eternal blessing when Jesus comes to take us into glory.

King Solomon, in his wisdom, wrote:

> “The end of a matter is better than its beginning, and patience is better than pride.” (Ecclesiastes 7:8)

Think about some project or responsibility you had starting out — maybe you thought, “This is going to be tough; this is going to require a lot of effort, a lot of thought, reliance on others…” and you might have felt a little overwhelmed. But Solomon said: how much better is the end of a matter, when the project is complete, and you can look back and say, “Yes, I did it.”

But he points out that it’s *patience* — waiting on the Lord with trust in His working — that is better than *pride*. Pride says, “I did it,” or “I could do this.” It’s not about you; it’s about what God does. Showing wisdom in life starts with faith in God’s working, and the promise that He will always bring good results — then giving Him the glory.

**2. Patience in the face of suffering**

James goes on to talk about patience in another area of life — suffering:

> “Brothers and sisters, as an example of patience in the face of suffering, take the prophets who spoke in the name of the Lord. As you know, we count as blessed those who have persevered. You have heard of Job’s perseverance and have seen what the Lord finally brought about. The Lord is full of compassion and mercy.” (James 5:10–11)

Job’s life turned around completely, and he was better off at the end of his suffering than before. James is really talking about two kinds of suffering: first, the hardships we go through in life — here, just be patient, because God is working through those hardships too, working all things together for our good. Second, suffering because of persecution for our faith — he refers to the Old Testament prophets, who suffered because they were faithful in their message from God.

The Apostle John, at the end of the first century of Christianity, was persecuted too, and he talked about patient endurance:

> “I, John, your brother and companion in the suffering and kingdom and patient endurance that are ours in Jesus, was on the island of Patmos because of the word of God and the testimony of Jesus.” (Revelation 1:9, paraphrased)

Patmos was a rocky little island off the coast of what is today Turkey. John was put there because of his belief in the word of God and the testimony of Jesus. Later, after Jesus showed him visions of the rest of world history and the hardships Christians would experience, Jesus tells them:

> “If anyone is to go into captivity, into captivity they will go. If anyone is to be killed with the sword, with the sword they will be killed. This calls for patient endurance and faithfulness on the part of God’s people.” (Revelation 13:10)

Even in suffering for our faith, this calls for patient endurance — put your trust in God.

**3. Patience toward others**

There’s also the kind of patience we’re to display toward others, and here we return to that very first verse we started with this morning. We do this because it shows Christian character. Paul wrote:

> “Therefore, as God’s chosen people, holy and dearly loved, clothe yourselves with… patience.” (Colossians 3:12)

It’s because of who we are — God’s holy and dearly loved people — that we show that Christian character to others.

Sometimes it’s hard to be patient with other people, especially if they’ve hurt you in some way. Going back to that proverb from Solomon — that the knowledge of salvation yields patience:

> “It is to one’s glory to overlook an offense.” (Proverbs 19:11)

In other words, be patient and forgive those who have offended you. Sometimes that’s hard to do, because we may have been really hurt, and we think, “Until I don’t feel hurt, I won’t forgive,” or “Unless they show me they’re sorry, or do something nice, I am not going to forgive.” Let me ask: is that the way God treats you? Is that how God forgives you? “Once they get their life in order and do good for me, then I’ll forgive”? No — God’s love is unconditional. God’s love is unending; it’s free and full. So He says: show the same patience and love I showed you, and freely forgive — and then you will find healing in your soul.

There’s another purpose for this too. Paul says:

> “Preach the word; be prepared in season and out of season; correct, rebuke and encourage — with great patience and careful instruction.” (2 Timothy 4:2)

We’re reminded that our purpose in showing Christian character and patience is to bring people to faith. We do that as we share God’s law and message about sin, but also give the encouragement of the love and forgiveness we have in Christ. Be patient with others.

## Conclusion

As you see, the Scriptures talk a lot about patience. Thank you for being patient with me this morning through this longer-than-usual message.

My encouragement is this: remember the patience God has for you and for others, and remember His persistence to always work His blessings.

May God help us live lives full of patience. Amen.
