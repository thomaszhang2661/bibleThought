# Sermon: Living a Life of Kindness

## Introduction: Where Kindness Really Comes From

We might think of ourselves as defined by what we do and how we treat others — and maybe we assume our kindness matters because if we're kind to someone, we'll get kindness back. But Paul points out something different: it wasn't our kindness that brought us the kindness of God. In fact, it was just the opposite — it was our wickedness, our disobedience, that brought the kindness of God, displayed in His sending Jesus into the world to save us. The kindness we now show is a response to the kindness already shown to us.

**Scripture: Titus 3** (Paul's letter to the pastor Titus):

> At one time we too were foolish, disobedient, deceived and enslaved by all kinds of passions and pleasures. We lived in malice and envy, being hated and hating one another. But when the kindness and love of God our Savior appeared, he saved us, not because of righteous things we had done, but because of his mercy. He saved us through the washing of rebirth and renewal by the Holy Spirit, whom he poured out on us generously through Jesus Christ our Savior, so that, having been justified by his grace, we might become heirs having the hope of eternal life. This is a trustworthy saying. And I want you to stress these things, so that those who have trusted in God may be careful to devote themselves to doing what is good. These things are excellent and profitable for everyone.

That is God's word about living a life of kindness — but even that comes to us because of the kindness of Jesus first.

## Gospel Account: The Canaanite Woman (Matthew 15)

Jesus showed that kindness to a woman who came to him seeking an answer to a problem with her daughter. She sought the Lord in prayer, but he didn't answer right away — it almost looked as though he was ignoring or rejecting her. In truth, God was testing her faith to draw her closer to him, to rely on his promise of help — and Jesus did answer, showing that his kindness is expressed in his care for us.

**Scripture: Matthew 15**:

> Leaving that place, Jesus withdrew to the region of Tyre and Sidon. A Canaanite woman from that vicinity came to him, crying out, "Lord, Son of David, have mercy on me! My daughter is demon-possessed and suffering terribly." Jesus did not answer a word. So his disciples came to him and urged him, "Send her away, for she keeps crying out after us." He answered, "I was sent only to the lost sheep of Israel." The woman came and knelt before him. "Lord, help me," she said. He replied, "It is not right to take the children's bread and toss it to the dogs." "Yes it is, Lord," she said. "Even the dogs eat the crumbs that fall from their master's table." Then Jesus said to her, "Woman, you have great faith! Your request is granted." And her daughter was healed at that moment.

Jesus is kind enough to hear our requests, answer our prayers, and care for us.

## Profession of Faith: "God Will Take Care of Me"

> I believe that no matter what this life brings my way, God knows about it and will take care of me. Come what may — health or sickness, riches or famine, life or death — I know God takes care of me in every situation. So I call this to mind, and I have hope: because of the Lord's great love we are not overwhelmed, for his compassions never fail; they are new every morning, great is his faithfulness. The Lord is good to those who have hope in him, to the one who seeks him; it is good to wait quietly for the deliverance of the Lord. Though trials may come our way, God shows compassion to us — so great is his unfailing love. I call on your name, O Lord, and even from the depths of despair, you hear my plea, my cry for relief. You come near when I call, and you say to me, "Do not fear." Because of the ultimate sacrifice of my Savior Jesus, my soul is safe in my heavenly Father's arms, and I have nothing to fear, because God takes care of me. May God continue to strengthen our faith in his care.

## Main Message: Living a Life of Kindness

### The Watermelon Illustration

"Jesus Watermelon" might sound like an odd sermon title — but it's simply an illustration to open the topic. Unlike many fruits you can eat as-is, you can't eat through a watermelon's thick, hard rind — but you can still enjoy it, and in a surprising number of ways. You might grab a big slice and gnaw at it, or cut it into small chunks and eat it more delicately. A little internet research turned up a chart listing six different ways to eat watermelon (putting sticks in it, blending it into a slushie, and so on); Martha Stewart claims there are 20 ways; this preacher found 21. And if you're still unsure how to go about it, there's always a YouTube video to walk you through it.

Why talk about watermelon? Because there are so many ways to approach something as simple as a piece of fruit — and Paul gives us a "shopping list" of fruit in Galatians: the fruit of the Spirit is love, joy, peace, forbearance (patience), and kindness. How do we take something on that list — kindness — and actually break it down into something we can practice? As part of this summer's series on living a fruitful life, today's focus is living a life of kindness, following Paul's guidance in Romans 11:

> Consider therefore the kindness and sternness of God: sternness to those who fell, but kindness to you, provided that you continue in his kindness.

The first word, "consider," translates a Greek word often rendered "behold" — meaning: look, take a good look, and take notes. So let's consider the kindness God has shown toward us.

### 1. Jacob: Blessed, Protected, and Saved

Jacob was a twin — his brother was Esau, sons of the patriarch Isaac. As the older son, Esau stood to receive the birthright: the responsibility of heading the family, along with God's promise that the Savior would come through their line. Jacob, jealous of his brother, devised a plan to take that birthright for himself. When Esau came in from hunting, starving, Jacob traded him a bowl of stew for the birthright — though it wasn't really Esau's alone to give; it had to come from their father Isaac. Jacob then disguised himself as Esau — animal fur on his arm, a disguised voice — and deceived his dying, blind father into giving him Esau's blessing.

When Esau found out, he was furious enough to threaten to kill his brother. Their mother Rebecca sent Jacob north, hundreds of miles away, to stay with relatives.

**The first dream, on the journey out**: Jacob lay down to rest one night and dreamed of a staircase reaching from earth to heaven, with angels going up and down it. At the top stood the Lord himself, who promised Jacob that he would be with him wherever he went, bless him, and bring him back safely to that land.

Jacob went on to Haran, met his relatives, and in time married twice (not God's blueprint for marriage, but it happened, and God still blessed those marriages). God gave him twelve sons, who became the heads of the twelve tribes of Israel, and blessed him with many possessions — herds, flocks, and servants.

After several years, Jacob decided to return and reconcile with Esau, worried about the old threat against his life. **The second dream, on the journey back**: Jacob wrestled with a man in a vision and said, "I will not let you go until you bless me." That man was God, and God blessed him again, renewing the promise to be with him and bring him safely home — which is exactly what happened. Jacob and Esau met with no hostility; they reconciled.

**Application**: Count your blessings. See the kindness of God in your own life the way Jacob learned to see it in his. Jacob was guided by God — out of his home, to his relatives, into a family, into blessing, and back again. It echoes something the Old Testament believer Job said about his own life:

> You gave me life and showed me kindness, and in your providence you watched over my spirit.

Could you say the same about your life? Almost certainly, yes. Look back and see how God has guided you — into a family, through education and training, into work, career, and vocation, bringing different people into your life. Through all of it, you can see God's blessing, because he was guiding you and providing for you the whole way.

In Jacob's life, he was never lacking. God sometimes provided in miraculous ways, but he was never truly short. God has done the same for us — through family, friends, jobs, training, and skill. There may have been lean seasons, tight budgets, but none of us have known the poverty that so much of the world has known. God has provided for us. (It's worth noting — some recent news coverage of foreign visitors to the U.S. for soccer matches showed just how impressed they were by conditions here, even as we sometimes find reasons to complain about what we lack.) When we truly look, we see how blessed we are.

**God also protects us**, as he protected Jacob traveling through unfamiliar territory under threat from his brother. The staircase dream, with angels ascending and descending, points to God's ongoing protection through his messengers. It calls to mind a verse from the hymn _"I Walk in Danger All the Way,"_ about angels shielding and defending the believer against every attack of the enemy, so that no fear or sorrow can overtake us as we walk protected all the way.

**Above all, God saves us from the greatest danger** — separation from him and eternal death because of our sin. God sent his Son to live the life of perfect obedience that we cannot live ourselves, and gives us that righteousness simply through faith in him. The sin that would separate us has been forgiven and removed — placed instead on Jesus, who bore its guilt and punishment on the cross, suffering God's wrath in our place so that we would never have to. Jesus's resurrection assures us that sin has been paid for, death's curse removed, and that we will live forever.

### 2. Give Credit to God's Grace

Why do we have all these blessings? What did we do to earn them? We often think, "I've been good," or "I did this or that, so I got something in return." But that's not why God's blessings come to us.

Another lesson from Jacob's life: give the credit to God's grace, not to ourselves. This is exactly what Jacob did — and it's the essence of God's kindness: his grace put into action in our lives, unearned. Jacob said it himself:

> I am unworthy of all the kindness and faithfulness you have shown your servant. I had only my staff when I crossed this Jordan, but now I have become two camps.

Jacob credited God's unearned grace for everything he had — and the same is true for us. It isn't our niceness, our obedience, our attempts at faithfulness that bring us God's blessings; it's entirely his undeserved love for us and for the whole world. We call this grace unconditional, because God has never set a standard we must meet to qualify for his blessing. God says through the prophet Hosea:

> I led them with cords of human kindness, with ties of love. To them I was like one who lifts a little child to the cheek, and I bent down to feed them.

These words picture God's unconditional grace — faithfully leading us, holding us close the way you'd hold a child to your cheek, bending down in humility to meet someone's need.

**And that love is for the whole world.** Paul, preaching to unbelievers, said:

> Yet he has not left himself without testimony: He has shown kindness by giving you rain from heaven and crops in their seasons; he provides you with plenty of food and fills your hearts with joy.

God's kindness is unconditional, and it's for everyone.

### 3. God's Kindness Never Fails

Solomon, reflecting on all God had done for his father David, described God's unfailing love this way:

> He gives his king great victories; he shows unfailing kindness to his anointed, to David and his descendants forever.

And through Jeremiah, God said:

> The Lord appeared to us in the past, saying, "I have loved you with an everlasting love; I have drawn you with unfailing kindness."

There may be seasons when we ask, "Where is God's kindness? What is he doing?" These verses remind us that his kindness never fails — nothing gets in the way of it.

**Joseph's example**: beaten and kidnapped by his own brothers, sold into slavery, made a servant in an Egyptian household, propositioned by his master's wife and falsely accused when he refused to be unfaithful to God, thrown into prison and forgotten for years. And yet:

> The Lord was with him; he showed him kindness and granted him favor in the eyes of the prison warden.

Joseph was eventually released through God's grace and became the second most powerful man in Egypt. The point: God's grace is unfailing. Nothing that happens to us can stop God's grace from blessing us — it is unearned, unconditional, and unfailing.

**But do we actually receive it that way?** The psalmist points to Israel's failure in Egypt:

> Our ancestors in Egypt did not consider your miracles; they did not remember your many kindnesses, and they rebelled by the sea.

Israel was enslaved in Egypt for over 400 years, was delivered through the ten plagues, watched the Red Sea split so they could escape the pursuing Egyptian army, drank water from a rock Moses struck in the desert, and ate food that appeared from the sky morning and evening — and still forgot all of it.

We say elephants never forget (who tested that, anyway?) — but people forget God's kindness all the time. Don't we forget how good God has been to us, and go looking for solutions apart from him?

Isaiah records God saying, of Israel's rebellion:

> In a surge of anger I hid my face from you for a moment,

but then finishes the thought:

> but with everlasting kindness I will have compassion on you, says the Lord your Redeemer.

Sometimes what feels like the absence of God's kindness is simply God drawing us back to himself, as Paul says elsewhere: the kindness of God leads us to repentance. God does not forget us, even when we forget him — his kindness and compassion remain.

### 4. Passing Kindness On to Others

Having considered God's kindness, we're now called to communicate it — to share it in common with others. Paul writes:

> As God's chosen people, holy and dearly loved, clothe yourselves with compassion and kindness.

Just as we sometimes dress to be noticed, God wants us to "wear" kindness so that people notice his kindness through us. Think of grade-school Show and Tell — that's how we pass on God's kindness: show it, and tell about it. A few scriptural examples of what that looks like in practice:

**1. Meeting practical needs.** When Paul was shipwrecked on a journey to Rome:

> The islanders showed us unusual kindness. They built a fire and welcomed us all, because it was raining and cold.

These islanders weren't believers — if they could show kindness like that, how much more should we, who know the kindness of God?

**2. Compassion for the vulnerable.** When David became king after Saul and Jonathan died in battle, he asked whether anyone remained from Saul's family, wanting to show kindness for Jonathan's sake. He learned of Mephibosheth, a young man left lame after being dropped by his caretaker as a child. Understandably fearful for his life as the last remnant of the previous king's line, Mephibosheth instead heard David say:

> Don't be afraid, for I will surely show you kindness for the sake of your father Jonathan. I will restore to you all the land that belonged to your grandfather Saul, and you will always eat at my table.

Picture the scene: a crippled man seated at the royal table alongside David's officials and generals — David's kindness toward someone with nothing to offer in return.

**3. Comfort for the grieving.** When the father of a man named Hanun died — a man who had once shown David kindness — David sent a delegation to express sympathy. We can do the same when we don't know what to say to someone who's grieving. Jesus himself modeled this at Lazarus's tomb, weeping with the family even though he knew he would raise Lazarus from the dead. Express sympathy and support for those who mourn.

**4. Standing by the afflicted.** When David's son Absalom led a rebellion and David fled Jerusalem, a foreigner named Ittai the Gittite chose to go with him. David urged him to go back:

> Why should you come along with us? Go back and stay with the new king. You are a foreigner, an exile from your homeland. You came only yesterday. Today shall I make you wander with us, when I don't even know where I'm going myself? Go back, and take your people with you. May the Lord show you kindness and faithfulness.

Ittai answered that wherever David went, whether to life or death, there his servant would be also. We show kindness by standing with those who are suffering, thinking of their needs rather than our own comfort — as a thankful response to the kindness God has shown us. Jonathan, David's friend, asked for exactly this kind of loyalty:

> Show me unfailing kindness like the Lord's kindness as long as I live.

**5. When you don't know what to say or do, pray.** Abraham's servant, sent on a difficult errand, prayed:

> Lord, God of my master Abraham, make me successful today, and show kindness to my master Abraham.

Prayer always works.

**6. Tell others about God's kindness.** Paul writes that God's purpose was:

> that in the coming ages he might show the incomparable riches of his grace, expressed in his kindness to us in Christ Jesus.

Proclaiming that grace was Paul's whole mission. Isaiah likewise says:

> I will tell of the kindness of the Lord, the deeds for which he is to be praised, according to all the Lord has done for us — the many good things he has done ... according to his compassion and many kindnesses.

That's what our praise and worship are for: proclaiming God's kindness in Jesus to others.

**7. Correction, too, can be kindness.** As the psalmist puts it:

> Let a righteous man strike me — that is a kindness; let him rebuke me — that is oil on my head.

Words of correction, rightly meant, are meant to bring someone to repentance and to the forgiveness found in Jesus — which is itself an act of kindness.

**8. Kindness that brings new life.** Ezra reminded the returning exiles:

> Though we are slaves, our God has not forsaken us in our bondage. He has shown us kindness in the sight of the kings of Persia: he has granted us new life to rebuild the house of our God and repair its ruins, and he has given us a wall of protection in Judah and Jerusalem.

All of God's kindness is aimed at giving us new life with him — surely something worth showing and telling others about.

## Conclusion

There are a lot of ways to eat a watermelon. There are a lot of ways to show the kindness of God. Thank you for your patience with a longer-than-usual message. Take this one last thought with you:

> Do not let kindness and truth leave you; bind them around your neck, write them on the tablet of your heart.

That's how we live a life of kindness — letting God's kindness live out through ours toward others. May God give us that fruit. Amen.

## Closing Prayer

We confess that toward others we fall short. Forgive us, for the sake of the kindness you have shown this world through Jesus our Savior — the same kindness that has been shown to us. The kindness of God is meant to lead us to repentance, to find the greatest gift of all: forgiveness in Jesus. You have it. May you show it to others. Amen. Thankful for all the kindness shown to us.
