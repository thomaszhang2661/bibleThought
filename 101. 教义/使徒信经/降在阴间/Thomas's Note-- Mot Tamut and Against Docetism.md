# He Descended into Hell: A Two-Part Inquiry into the Apostles' Creed and Scripture

---

## Introduction

"I believe… in Jesus Christ… who descended into hell, and on the third day rose again from the dead."

This phrase has provoked enduring theological controversy throughout Christian history. This article argues that much of the confusion arises from a failure to distinguish two separate questions:

**The first question**: What does the clause "descended into hell" mean within the Apostles' Creed—within its own historical context and narrative sequence? This is a question of creedal interpretation, and must be answered by applying the historical-grammatical method within the Creed's own context. 

**The second question**: What actually happened between Christ's death and resurrection? This is a question of biblical exegesis, requiring a synthesis of multiple relevant texts.

These two questions are closely related but must not be conflated. The first is the premise; the second is the extension. Many historical debates—including the division between Lutheranism and the Reformed tradition—have remained intractable precisely because each side has used material that answers the second question to answer the first.

---

# Part One: What Does "Descended into Hell" Mean in the Apostles' Creed?

## I. Three Questions That Must Be Answered in Order

To understand this clause, three historical questions must be answered in sequence:

**Why was this clause added historically? What did those who added it mean? Is there scriptural support for what they meant?**

Only by answering these three questions first can we assess whether the later readings of the various traditions are faithful to the clause itself.

---

## II. Historical Formation: Why It Was Added and What It Meant

### The Clause's Historical Timeline

The earliest extant Western document explicitly containing this clause is the Creed of Aquileia, as recorded by Tyrannius Rufinus (c. 345–411) in his _Commentary on the Apostles' Creed_ (_Commentarius in symbolum apostolorum_), written around AD 400. The relevant Latin text reads:

> _Crucifixus sub Pontio Pilato, et sepultus; descendit ad inferna; tertia die resurrexit a mortuis._ (He was crucified under Pontius Pilate, and was buried; he descended into hell; on the third day he rose from the dead.)

Rufinus simultaneously noted that the Roman Church's creed did not contain this clause, writing:

> "But this clause is not added in the Creed of the Roman Church… In other places, as far as I am aware, some additions have been made because of certain heretics, with the intention of excluding novel teachings." (_Symb._ §18)

This statement itself indicates that the clause was added in response to specific heresies.

**Timeline of Transmission**

|Date|Document|Notes|
|---|---|---|
|c. 359|Fourth Sirmium Formula|Earliest Eastern appearance; rejected due to Arian associations|
|c. 400|Rufinus, Creed of Aquileia|Earliest Western record; absent from Roman creed|
|5th century|Athanasian Creed|Contains descent clause; modern scholars date it to 5th-century southern Gaul|
|c. 650|Gallican Sacramentary|_descendit ad inferna_|
|c. 710–714|Pirminius|Earliest text closest to the complete Apostles' Creed|
|8th–11th century|Western Church|Gradually universally accepted|

This clause was never formally ratified by any ecumenical council. It entered the Creed through gradual liturgical practice—not by top-down theological decree.

### The Theological Motivation: Two Heresies

**I. Against Docetism**

Docetism (from the Greek δοκεῖν, "to seem") denied that Christ truly had a physical body, holding that he only appeared to suffer and die. Rufinus explicitly noted that additions to the creed were made "because of certain heretics." Affirming that Christ genuinely entered the realm of the dead is the most direct refutation of this heresy: he did not merely appear to die, but truly died—so truly that his soul entered the very place where the dead go.

**II. Against Apollinarianism**

Apollinaris of Laodicea (c. 310–390) held that Christ lacked a rational human soul (_nous_), with the divine Logos directly occupying that place. This heresy was condemned at the Council of Constantinople in 381. Gregory of Nazianzus's decisive argument was: "What has not been assumed cannot be redeemed." Affirming that Christ's **soul** truly entered the realm of death is therefore a declaration that he possessed a genuine human soul—and that this soul truly experienced death.

**What Is Apollinarianism?**

Apollinarianism is a fourth-century Christological heresy proposed by Apollinaris of Laodicea. In attempting to resolve the question of how Christ could be both fully God and fully human, Apollinaris proposed:

> Christ had a genuine human **body** and **animal soul** (the animating life-force), but no human **rational soul** (_nous_). In its place, the divine Logos directly occupied that position.

In brief: Christ = human body + animal soul + **divine Logos** (replacing the human rational soul).

Gregory of Nazianzus demolished this with a single line:

> **"What has not been assumed cannot be redeemed."** _(Quod non assumptum, non sanatum.)_

If Christ had no complete human soul, then the human soul was never taken up by him—and therefore was never redeemed. This directly undermines the completeness of the atonement. The heresy was formally condemned at the First Council of Constantinople in 381.

Its connection to "descended into hell" is direct: affirming that Christ's **soul** truly entered the realm of death declares that he possessed a real human soul—one that genuinely experienced death—and that the redemption wrought by this soul is therefore complete.

Both of these controversies point toward the same theological declaration: Christ's death was real and complete—not merely physical death, but the soul genuinely entering the realm of death.

### The Original Meaning of the Vocabulary

The Greek version of the Apostles' Creed uses **ᾅδης (Hades)**; the Latin versions use **inferos/inferna**. Regardless of the variant, the root word **infernus/inferos** means "the lower place," "the realm of the dead"—cognate with English _inferior_ (below)—and **carries no connotation of punitive hell (gehenna)**.

The English tradition translates this as "hell," but modern English "hell" carries strong connotations of punishment, far removed from the original meaning—which is why many modern English versions of the Creed now render it as "the dead" or "the realm of the dead." The Chinese rendering 阴间 (the shadowy realm) is comparatively apt.

The Anglican Church, in its _Worship Supplement_, permits any congregation to omit "he descended into hell" or substitute "he went into the place of departed spirits," declaring the two phrases to be of equivalent meaning within the Creed. This official provision is significant: the Anglican Church has institutionally acknowledged that this clause refers to Christ going to the place where the dead dwell.

### The 7th–8th Century Church's Understanding

The historical-grammatical method requires us to begin with what the Church understood by this clause when it was widely adopted—not to retroject later theological frameworks onto it.

**The Latin Transmission Path**

The clause spread through specific geographical channels in the seventh century: the _Bobbio Missal_ (Ireland, c. 630–640); the _Bangor Antiphonary_ (Ireland, c. 680–691); the _Gallican Missal_ (c. 700); transmitted southward to Rome by the mid-seventh century; and incorporated into the fullest extant manuscript of the Apostles' Creed by Pirminius (c. 710–714).

**Contemporary Eastern Orthodox Liturgical Texts**

Metropolitan Hilarion Alfeyev, in _Christ the Conqueror of Hell_ (St. Vladimir's Seminary Press, 2009), notes that Christ's descent into Hades is referenced more than fifty times in the Orthodox Holy Friday and Holy Saturday liturgies. The authors of these texts include:

- **Romanos the Melodist** (c. 485–560), whose Kontakion 26 declares:
    
    > "You stood at the tomb of Lazarus, O Lord, and raised the four-days-dead… **binding Hades**, O Mighty One…"
    
    And Kontakion 27, with Christ himself speaking:
    
    > "For this I came, to raise Adam and Adam's descendants… **destroying the power of Hades with my voice**."
    
- **Sophronius of Jerusalem** (c. 560–638), Patriarch of Jerusalem in the seventh century, whose Holy Saturday poetry describes Christ entering the realm of the dead after his death.
    
- **Andrew of Crete** (c. 660–740), whose _Great Canon_ explicitly links Christ's descent to the realm of the dead.
    

The central Paschal troparion sung throughout this period:

> "Christ is risen from the dead, **trampling down death by death**, and upon those in the tombs bestowing life."

**The 8th-Century _Book of Cerne_ Drama**

The earliest extant text that may have been used for liturgical performance is the _Harrowing of Hell_ drama in the _Book of Cerne_ (c. 820–840, Cambridge University Library MS Ll.1.10), fols. 98v–99v. This fifty-five-line Latin drama has three voices—Adam, Eve, and a narrator (distinguished by red ink)—with a chorus of _antiqui iusti_ (the ancient righteous), depicting Christ entering the realm of the dead and releasing the captives. Scholar David N. Dumville has established that this drama was composed from a pseudo-Augustinian sermon and the _Gospel of Nicodemus_ tradition of the descent (_Journal of Theological Studies_ 23/2, 1972).

The drama is entirely spatial and ontological in character—Christ **enters** the place where the dead dwell and brings out the captives. This is neither an inner experience on the cross nor a triumphant proclamation after the resurrection, but a real event occurring after death and before resurrection.

**Conclusion**: The Calvinist reading ("inner experience of divine judgment on the cross") and the standard Lutheran reading ("triumphant proclamation after the vivification") are both post-Reformation theological developments. When this clause was added and widely adopted, the Church's prevalent understanding was: Christ truly entered the realm of the dead, and this occurred after his death and before his resurrection.

## III. Scriptural Support: The Biblical Basis for What the Clause Meant

The third historical question: is there scriptural support for what the 4th–8th century Church meant—namely, that Christ's soul genuinely entered the realm of death?

The answer is yes. The evidence is presented below in order of strength.

### The Most Direct Evidence: Acts 2:27, 31

Peter, in his Pentecost sermon, cites Psalm 16:10:

> "For you will not abandon my soul to Hades, or let your Holy One see corruption." (Acts 2:27)

> "He foresaw and spoke about the resurrection of the Christ, that he was not abandoned to Hades, nor did his flesh see corruption." (Acts 2:31)

Peter's argument presupposes that Christ's soul **did enter** Hades, and then was **not left there**—for if the soul had never entered, there would be nothing to say about "not being left." This is the most direct New Testament statement supporting the real entry of the soul into Hades.

It is worth noting that this is precisely the scriptural text upon which the early Church fathers relied when adding and using this clause. Rufinus cites Psalm 16:10 in his commentary; Luther's Torgau sermon likewise grounds its argument in Psalm 16 and Acts 2—not in 1 Peter 3. This shows that from the clause's earliest insertion, Acts 2 was its most natural biblical anchor.

### Presuppositional Evidence: Romans 10:6–7

> "Who will descend into the abyss? (that is, to bring Christ up from the dead)"

Paul's argument presupposes that Christ genuinely was in the abyss—held by death—and was then brought out by God. "Descend into the abyss" and "bring Christ up from the dead" are placed in parallel, echoing the "pangs of death" (ὠδῖνας τοῦ θανάτου) of Acts 2:24.

### Supporting Evidence: Ephesians 4:9

> "In saying, 'He ascended,' what does it mean but that he had also descended into the lower regions of the earth?"

The contrast of descent and ascent, if "the lower regions" refers to Hades, produces a narrative arc that perfectly matches the Creed's sequence: descended into hell → rose again → ascended into heaven. It must be acknowledged that this verse is ambiguous—many modern translations read it as a reference to the Incarnation—so it can only serve as supporting rather than primary evidence. That said, the context (Paul citing Psalm 68:18 about Christ's triumph and the spoils of conquest) suggests a spiritual battle framework, which may favor the descent-into-Hades reading over the Incarnation reading.

### Old Testament Background: Psalm 16:10

> "For you will not abandon my soul to Sheol, or let your holy one see corruption."

Peter in Acts 2:27 explicitly applies this psalm to Christ—the soul entered Sheol/Hades, then came out in the resurrection. This is the most direct scriptural bridge connecting the Old Testament concept of Sheol to Christ's descent into the realm of the dead.

### Old Testament Narrative Presupposition: 1 Samuel 28:15

> "Samuel said to Saul, 'Why have you disturbed me by bringing me **up**?' (עָלָה, _alah_)"

Samuel was called up from **below**—the narrative presupposes that the souls of the dead dwell beneath, in Sheol. This is not a direct argument but the narrative background of the Old Testament's understanding of the reality of the realm of death.

### Summary

The scriptural evidence supporting the reading that "the soul genuinely entered the realm of death" is strongest in Acts 2, followed by the presuppositional argument of Romans 10:7, with Ephesians 4:9 providing supplementary support despite its ambiguity. These texts—especially Acts 2—are the very scriptures upon which the Church that added and adopted this clause relied, and are the texts that both Rufinus and Luther invoked in their respective interpretations.

## IV. The Creed's Internal Evidence: Narrative Sequence

The Creed's own narrative sequence is the most important—and most frequently overlooked—internal evidence:

> Suffered → Died → **Was buried** → **Descended into hell** → Rose again on the third day → Ascended into heaven

**"Descended into hell" is plainly before the resurrection.** This directly determines the following points.

**First**, it describes what happened after death and before resurrection—not something on the cross, and not something after the resurrection.

**Second**, it stands alongside "was buried" without repeating it. Burial refers to the body being placed in the tomb; descended into hell refers to the soul entering the realm of death. Together they bear the two dimensions of complete death.

**Third, Calvin's reading contravenes this sequence.** If "descended into hell" refers to bearing divine judgment on the cross, it ought to appear before or alongside "died," not after "was buried." David P. Scaer makes this criticism precisely: the word order places the descent after the burial, not after the death (Scaer, _JETS_ 35/1, 1992, p. 96).

**Fourth, the Lutheran reading also creates tension with the word order.** Lutheranism places the descent after the vivification (ζῳοποιηθείς), treating it as the first act of Christ's state of exaltation. But the Creed plainly places "descended into hell" before the resurrection—a tension that requires additional explanation.

It is worth noting that Luther himself, in the Torgau sermon, said: "before He rose and ascended into heaven, **while He still lay in the grave**, He also descended to hell" (_WA_ 37:62–72; trans. Rydecki, 2023). This aligns with the Creed's word order and actually stands in tension with the later standard Lutheran position of locating the descent after the vivification.

Taking the sequence at face value, the most natural reading is: the descent into hell marks the completion of Christ's state of humiliation—an event after death and before resurrection, in which the soul genuinely entered the realm of death.

## V. Assessment of the Traditions

### The Two Propositions Behind the Debate

All orthodox traditions affirm the following two propositions:

**Proposition 1**: Christ genuinely bore the full debt of human sin and its consequences, including complete separation from God—"My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" (Mark 15:34; cf. Genesis 2, _mot tamut_—"dying you shall die").

**Proposition 2**: Christ conquered death. The resurrection is the genuine conquest of the power of death.

The real question at issue is: **which proposition does the Apostles' Creed's clause "descended into hell" primarily emphasize?**

|Tradition|What the clause emphasizes|
|---|---|
|Calvin / Reformed|Proposition 1—Christ bearing complete judgment and forsakenness on the cross|
|Lutheranism|Proposition 2—Christ's triumphant proclamation to the devil after the resurrection|
|Eastern Orthodoxy|Both—entering death in order to shatter it from within|
|This article|Proposition 1—Christ bearing the complete death of _mot tamut_|

This article agrees with Calvin that the clause emphasizes Proposition 1, but with a fundamental difference: Calvin locates it on the cross, as an inner subjective experience of the soul; this article holds that it is located after death and before resurrection, as the genuine entry of the soul into the realm of death. Calvin gave Proposition 1 the right theological weight but placed it at the wrong moment.

Lutheranism holds that the clause emphasizes Proposition 2. The problem with this reading is not Proposition 2 itself, but that Proposition 2 is already fully expressed by the clause "rose again on the third day." If "descended into hell" only reiterates Proposition 2, then the relationship between it and the resurrection clause requires further explanation.

**The most natural reading is: the Creed uses "descended into hell" to complete Proposition 1, and "rose again on the third day" to declare Proposition 2. Each clause carries its own theological weight, and together they constitute the complete narrative of Christ's redemptive work.**

### Lutheranism: Answering the Wrong Question

The core problem with the Lutheran reading is that it imports the exegetical conclusions of 1 Peter 3 directly into the interpretation of the creedal clause, conflating the two. This is a category error—the Creed is not a commentary on 1 Peter, and the creedal clause must first be interpreted within its own context.

The historical-grammatical method requires that we first read the clause in its own context: the historical circumstances of its insertion into the Creed, and its place within the Creed's narrative sequence. The Lutheran reading is problematic on both counts.

**Scaer's Central Proposition: _Christus crucifixus is Christus victor_**

Scaer summarizes his theological position with this Latin phrase: the crucified Christ is the victorious Christ. For him, the descent, the resurrection appearances, and the session at the right hand together constitute Christ's unified proclamation of victory in three distinct but related realms—hell, earth, and heaven. Removing the descent clause, he argues, removes the one explicit declaration of Christ's victory over Satan from the Creed (Scaer, p. 99).

This proposition is itself profound—cross and victory are inseparable; Christ's death is his victory. However, Scaer ties this theological insight to the temporal judgment that **the descent occurred after the resurrection**, and this article holds that this tie is unnecessary. The proposition "the crucified Christ is the victorious Christ" stands equally within this article's framework: Christ entered under the power of death (descended to the lowest point), then came out of it (resurrection, ascended to the highest)—this is a more thorough victory, not a weaker submission. Victory does not need to be proclaimed in advance; the resurrection itself is the proclamation of victory.

Scaer is a distinctive voice within Lutheranism. He systematically deploys the word-order argument against Calvin—an argument this article fully accepts. He also specifically notes that Luther's Torgau sermon does not argue from 1 Peter 3 but from Matthew 12:22–32 (the parable of the strong man overcome by one stronger)—which indirectly supports this article's contention that the creedal clause and the exegesis of 1 Peter must be distinguished. Nevertheless, Scaer ultimately places the descent after the resurrection. This article holds that his word-order argument, if applied consistently, ought to lead to the conclusion that the descent occurred **before** the resurrection—a step he did not take. To reach the conclusion that he did not reach using his own argumentative tools is this article's most honest engagement with his work.

### The Reformed (Calvin): Right Instinct, Wrong Timing

Calvin, in the _Institutes of the Christian Religion_ (II.16.10), interprets "descended into hell" as Christ's bearing of God's complete judgment and forsakenness on the cross—the very essence of hell's torment. His theological concern is genuine, but the reading faces two insurmountable problems:

**The word-order problem**: If the descent refers to suffering on the cross, it ought to appear before or alongside "died," not after "was buried" (Scaer, p. 96).

**The redundancy problem**: If the descent merely reiterates cross-suffering, it overlaps heavily with "suffered," and the clause loses independent theological content.

Calvin's instinct is correct—this clause concerns a dimension of death deeper than mere physical burial. But he located this deeper death at the wrong moment and substituted an inner subjective experience for the genuine entry of the soul into the realm of death.

### Eastern Orthodoxy: Most Consistent with the Historical Understanding

The Eastern Orthodox reading of this clause is, of all the traditions, the most consistent with how the Church understood it when the clause was added. Christ's descent into Hades (Κατάβασις εἰς ᾅδου) is the central theme of Orthodox Paschal theology: Christ actively entered the realm of the power of death, shattering it from within, releasing those imprisoned by death. This coheres with the Creed's word order, the historical context, and the _mot tamut_ framework.

### Roman Catholicism: Broadly Consistent

Roman Catholicism holds that Christ descended to bring the Old Testament saints from the _limbus patrum_ into heaven (cf. Thomas Aquinas, _Summa Theologiae_ III, Q.52). This reading accepts the premise that Christ genuinely entered the realm of the dead, and is broadly consistent with the framework of this article. The disagreement concerns the scope and purpose of the descent rather than its basic character.

## VI. The Old Testament Theological Foundation: _Mot Tamut_

### The Death Sentence in Genesis

This clause has a deeper scriptural-theological root, tracing back to Genesis 2:17:

**מוֹת תָּמוּת (_mot tamut_)**

This is the Hebrew infinitive absolute construction, in which a verb is intensified by the repetition of its root. It translates literally as "dying you shall die," conveying the force of "you shall most certainly and completely die." This is not a casual warning. It is the most comprehensive declaration of death possible—encompassing not only physical death but the soul's entry into the realm of death and complete severance from God, the source of life.

This declaration forms the starting point of the entire biblical narrative of redemption. True redemption must squarely address the full weight of _mot tamut_—the Redeemer must enter this complete death in order to conquer it from within.

"Descended into hell" is therefore not merely a historical theological declaration but the direct response to the Genesis death sentence: burial bears the physical dimension of death; descended into hell bears the dimension of the soul. Together they constitute the complete bearing of _mot tamut_. "Rose again on the third day" is then the act of coming out of that uttermost depth—the genuine conquest.

### Sheol: The Old Testament Image of the Realm of Death

The Hebrew שְׁאוֹל (Sheol) is the dwelling of all the dead—a dark, silent place, cut off from God: "For in death there is no remembrance of you; in Sheol who will give you praise?" (Psalm 6:5). Yet the Old Testament contains an internal tension: God's sovereignty extends everywhere—"If I make my bed in Sheol, you are there!" (Psalm 139:8). This tension drove the ongoing development of thought about the afterlife, culminating in the Second Temple period's mature understanding of distinct regions within Hades for the righteous and the unrighteous (Luke 16).

### A Devotional Reflection: The Image of Substitutionary Atonement

After the historical and exegetical arguments, one Old Testament image deserves to be offered as a devotional reflection—not as direct proof, but as a theological pointer.

Genesis 3:21 records that after the fall of Adam and Eve, "the Lord God made for Adam and for his wife garments of skins and clothed them." These skins came from a slaughtered animal—the first substitutionary image in Scripture: a life given in death, to cover human shame.

God declared "dying you shall die," then used death to cover the consequences of death. This image points toward the ultimate atonement: from the first garment of skin in Eden, to the cross at Golgotha, to "descended into hell," to "rose again on the third day"—the narrative of how God directly bore and conquered the death he himself had declared.

---

## VII. Conclusion of Part One

Drawing together the historical context, the original meaning of the vocabulary, the Creed's narrative sequence, and the _mot tamut_ framework, the most natural meaning of the Apostles' Creed's clause "descended into hell" is:

> **After his death, Christ's soul genuinely entered the realm of the dead (Hades), before the resurrection. Burial demonstrates that the body truly died; descended into hell demonstrates that the soul also truly entered under the power of death. The resurrection is the act of coming out of that deepest place—the genuine victory.**

This reading:

- Fully accords with the Creed's narrative sequence
- Requires no symbolic reinterpretation of Hades
- Coheres with the historical-theological purpose of the clause's insertion (countering Docetism and Apollinarianism)
- Gives "descended into hell" theological content independent of "was buried"
- Gives "rose again on the third day" its full theological weight

The Creed's narrative rhythm is: **descent to the lowest point, ascent to the highest**. "Descended into hell" is the nadir of that arc—the precondition for resurrection to be a genuine victory.

# Part Two: What Actually Happened Between Christ's Death and Resurrection?

## I. The Nature of the Question

Part One addressed the meaning of the creedal clause itself, concluding: the clause declares that Christ's soul genuinely entered the realm of death. This is sufficient as a confession of faith.

Part Two poses a different question—one of biblical exegesis: **how does Scripture describe what Jesus experienced during that time? Where did he go? What did he do?**

Scripture provides several clues to this question but offers no systematic and complete account. The rich readings of the various traditions—triumphant proclamation, releasing the Old Testament saints, bearing divine judgment—are all theological edifices built upon these clues. This article does not attempt to definitively resolve the question, but to survey the relevant texts and assess the strength of each piece of evidence.

---

## II. The Clearest Text: Acts 2:27, 31

This is the strongest evidence in Part Two. Peter, in his Pentecost sermon, cites Psalm 16:10:

> "For you will not abandon my soul to Hades, or let your Holy One see corruption." (Acts 2:27)

> "He foresaw and spoke about the resurrection of the Christ, that he was not abandoned to Hades, nor did his flesh see corruption." (Acts 2:31)

Peter's argument is logically clear:

1. David in Psalm 16:10 prophesied that someone's soul would not be **left** in Hades
2. David himself died and his soul **was left** in Hades (his tomb still stood, 2:29)
3. Therefore this prophecy points to Christ
4. Christ's soul **entered** Hades, then was **not left there**—the resurrection

Two things are confirmed here:

**First**, Christ's soul after death genuinely entered Hades—"not left" presupposes "having entered."

**Second**, this is the state between death and resurrection—Peter speaks of Christ being held by "the pangs of death" (ὠδῖνας τοῦ θανάτου, 2:24) before his resurrection, then released by God.

**The most important contribution of this text**: it confirms that Christ's soul after death entered Hades and was held there by the power of death until the resurrection. This is Scripture's most direct description of "what he experienced during that time"—he was in the realm of death, held by death.

---

## III. Why Lutheranism Insists on Triumph Rather Than Captivity: An Important Theological Distinction

Acts 2:24 plainly states that Christ was held by "the pangs of death" (ὠδῖνας τοῦ θανάτου) and then released by God. This is a direct scriptural statement. Yet Lutheranism firmly refuses to understand the descent into hell as captivity by death, insisting instead on the triumphant proclamation reading. There is theological logic behind this, but it is worth examining critically.

### The Lutheran Concern: The Completeness of the Atonement

The core of Lutheran soteriology is that Christ **completely finished** the work of redemption on the cross. John 19:30—"It is finished" (τετέλεσται)—is the key anchor of Lutheran theology.

If Christ's descent into hell was captivity by death, Lutheranism fears this implies:

> After the cross, Christ remained in some passive, imprisoned state—was the atoning work truly completed on the cross?

This concern is directly connected to the Hamburg Controversy (1544). Aepinus had argued that the descent was a continuation of the atonement—Christ continuing to bear God's judgment in hell to complete the redemption. The _Formula of Concord_, Article IX, explicitly condemned this heresy. Lutheranism therefore remains highly vigilant toward any "passive descent" reading, fearing it provides ammunition for Aepinus's position.

Additionally, Lutheranism has a pastoral concern: if Christ descended as a prisoner, believers might ask—how is he different from any ordinary dead person? Lutheranism declares: Christ entered hell as Lord, not as prisoner. The _Formula of Concord_, Article IX, explicitly states the purpose of this doctrine is to give believers comfort: "hell and the devil cannot take captive or injure us and all who believe."

### This Concern Rests on a Confusion

However, the Lutheran concern is built on a conflation that can be clarified.

**Being held captive by death** and **the atonement being incomplete** are two entirely different things.

The "pangs of death" holding Christ in Acts 2:24 describes the real state of death itself—the state in which any dead person exists. This is not additional punishment, not continued suffering, and certainly not a continuation of the atonement. Just as a person's burial after death is not additional punishment but simply the real condition of death, the soul being held in Hades is simply the real condition of death.

The atonement being completed on the cross, and the soul genuinely existing under the power of Hades after death, are entirely compatible:

||Held by death (Acts 2:24)|The Lutheran concern|
|---|---|---|
|Atonement completed on|The cross (John 19:30)|The cross (John 19:30)|
|Nature of the descent|The real condition of death|If passive, atonement incomplete?|
|Relationship to the resurrection|Resurrection is the declaration of victory|Triumph proclaimed before resurrection|

Indeed, if the atonement was fully completed on the cross, then the soul being held by death is precisely the moment when **death itself does not yet know it has been conquered**—the resurrection is the moment of public declaration, the decisive reversal. Being held by death and then coming out of it is a more thorough victory, not a weaker submission.

### The Tension in Luther's Own Sermon

Most significantly, Luther in the Torgau sermon said:

> "For before He rose and ascended into heaven, **while He still lay in the grave**, He also descended to hell, in order that He might also deliver us out from there who should have lain captive therein." (_WA_ 37:62–72)

"While He still lay in the grave"—this is **before** the resurrection, precisely the moment the Creed's word order describes, and is much closer to the image of being held by death than to a triumphant proclamation after the vivification.

Luther himself did not fully resolve this tension in his sermon. The _Formula of Concord_, Article IX, subsequently required "no disputation concerning it, but that it should be believed and taught only in the simplest manner"—this humble position actually acknowledges the complexity of the question, without mandating a specific temporal reading.

This reveals one thing: the **standard Lutheran position** (triumphant proclamation after the vivification) stands in internal tension with **Luther's own sermon** (descended while still in the tomb). The _Formula of Concord_ chose to handle this tension with "no disputation" rather than resolving it by doctrinal decree.

---

## IV. A Difficult Text: 1 Peter 3:18–19

> "Being put to death in the flesh but made alive in the spirit, in which he went and proclaimed to the spirits in prison."

This is historically the most contested text, with several core questions that remain unresolved:

**Question 1: The temporal question**

This text does not specify whether the proclamation occurred after death and before resurrection, or after the resurrection. The antecedent of "in which" (ἐν ᾧ) is disputed. Lutheranism places it after the vivification, treating it as an act of the state of exaltation. This temporal sequence cannot be determined with certainty from the text itself.

**Question 2: Who are "the spirits in prison"?**

Two main readings: (1) the souls of those who died in the days of Noah who were disobedient; (2) the fallen angels of Genesis 6:1–4 (the tradition of 1 Enoch). Both have scholarly support.

**Question 3: What kind of proclamation is ἐκήρυξεν?**

This verb in the New Testament typically carries the force of authoritative proclamation rather than evangelistic appeal, distinct from the verb for preaching the gospel (εὐαγγελίζω). Most scholars hold this to be a proclamation of judgment or triumph, not an offer of a second chance of repentance for the dead.

**Scaer's Special Argument**

It is worth noting that David P. Scaer has advanced a more refined argument than standard Lutheranism. He argues that 1 Pet 3:18–22 was itself **written around an existing creedal formula**, because its structure closely resembles the Apostles' Creed: death → glorification → proclamation to the prison → resurrection → ascension (Scaer, _JETS_ 35/1, 1992, pp. 93–94).

If Scaer's argument holds, then the relationship between the Creed and 1 Peter is not the "two independent levels" this article proposes, but rather that 1 Peter itself is an expansion of the creedal formula. This is a view this article must directly address.

However, Scaer's argument contains an internal circularity: he uses the structure of 1 Pet 3:18–22 to prove that the Creed has apostolic-era roots, and then uses the Creed to interpret 1 Pet 3:18–22. Even if 1 Pet 3:18–22 was indeed written around some creedal framework, one cannot conclude from this that **the creedal clause is describing the specific content of 1 Pet 3:18–19**—structural similarity does not entail equivalence of meaning.

More importantly, even granting the structural similarity between 1 Pet 3:18–22 and the Creed, the creedal clause, as a concise liturgical confession of faith, must have its original meaning determined within its own historical context—it cannot be replaced by the richer theological narration of the Petrine epistle.

**Conclusion**: 1 Peter 3:18–19 describes something that happened, but the temporal sequence, the audience, and the nature of the proclamation are all difficult to determine. It cannot serve as the primary evidence for anchoring the temporal sequence, but neither can it be excluded from discussion.

---

## V. A Text Requiring Further Investigation: Ephesians 4:9

> "In saying, 'He ascended,' what does it mean but that he had also descended into the lower regions of the earth?" (κατέβη εἰς τὰ κατώτερα μέρη τῆς γῆς)

This verse has three main readings:

**Reading 1: Descent into Hades** (into the realm of the dead after death and before resurrection)

**Reading 2: The Incarnation** (the descent from heaven to earth)

**Reading 3: Pentecost** (the descent of the Holy Spirit)

Many modern translations (ESV, NIV, NLT) read τὰ κατώτερα μέρη τῆς γῆς as "the earth," favoring the Incarnation reading. But if understood as Hades, the contrast of descent and ascent in this verse perfectly matches the Creed's narrative arc: descended into hell → ascended into heaven.

The context is Paul citing Psalm 68:18 about Christ's triumph: Christ like an ancient conquering king, ascending on high with captives. Paul reasons: if he ascended, he must once have descended. If the descent refers to entering the deepest place of death, then the ascent is the most complete triumph. The context of spiritual warfare and the plundering of captives may favor the descent-into-Hades reading over the Incarnation reading.

**Conclusion**: This verse is ambiguous and cannot stand alone as strong evidence, but the descent-into-Hades reading has scholarly support beyond the Incarnation reading, and coheres with the overall biblical-theological framework.

---

## VI. Presuppositional Evidence: Romans 10:6–7

> "Who will descend into the abyss? (that is, to bring Christ up from the dead)"

The context is Paul's argument for justification by faith—faith does not require any extreme human act, because Christ has already accomplished everything. Paul's argument presupposes:

- Christ **once** descended (Incarnation and death)
- Christ **once** descended into the abyss (the realm of death)
- Christ **once** rose from the dead

"Who will descend into the abyss to bring Christ up from the dead"—here "descend into the abyss" and "bring Christ up from the dead" are placed in parallel, presupposing that Christ was genuinely in the abyss, held there by death, then brought out by God. This echoes "the pangs of death" (ὠδῖνας τοῦ θανάτου) of Acts 2:24 and "not abandoned to Hades" of Acts 2:27, supporting the conclusion that Christ was genuinely under the power of Hades before the resurrection.

---

## VII. Related Background Texts

**Luke 23:43: Paradise Within Hades**

> "Today you will be with me in paradise."

In the mature theological understanding of the Second Temple period, Hades has internal distinctions: the righteous dwell in "Abraham's bosom" or "paradise," the unrighteous in a place of torment (Luke 16:19–31). Both are within Hades. Therefore, "today you will be with me in paradise" describes Christ's soul entering Hades, but on the side where the righteous dwell—fully consistent with Acts 2:27's "soul entered Hades then came out in resurrection."

**Luke 23:46**

> "Father, into your hands I commit my spirit!"

Christ committing his spirit into the Father's hands at death is not contradicted by the soul entering Hades—within the Second Temple framework, the righteous region of Hades (paradise) remains under God's care, as Psalm 139:8's "if I make my bed in Sheol, you are there" provides the theological backdrop.

**Revelation 1:17–18**

> "I died, and behold I am alive forevermore, and I have the keys of Death and Hades."

Christ holds the keys of Death and Hades—presupposing that he entered and conquered that place.

**1 Samuel 28:15**

> "Samuel said to Saul, 'Why have you disturbed me by bringing me up?'"

Samuel was called up from **below**—the Old Testament's narrative presupposition that the souls of the dead dwell beneath, in Sheol.

---

## VIII. The Traditions' Readings in Part Two

Once the two questions are clearly distinguished, the readings of the various traditions each have their legitimate place in Part Two:

**Eastern Orthodoxy**: Christ entered Hades, shattered death from within, released the captives. This most naturally coheres with the narrative of Acts 2 and with the 7th–8th century liturgical tradition.

**Roman Catholicism**: Christ went to the _limbus patrum_ and brought the Old Testament saints into heaven. This is a specific reading of Acts 2 and Ephesians 4:8 ("he led a host of captives").

**Lutheranism**: Christ proclaimed victory to the devil as conqueror (1 Peter 3:18–19; Colossians 2:15). This answers the question of what Christ **did** there—a legitimate theological reading—but as argued in Part One, this is not the primary content of the creedal clause.

**The Reformed (Calvin)**: Christ bore God's judgment and forsakenness on the cross. This answers the question of what the creedal clause emphasizes (Part One), rather than what happened after Christ's death (Part Two). Within Part Two's framework, Mark 15:34 describes what happened **before** death on the cross, not after.

---

## IX. Provisional Conclusion of Part Two

Drawing together the above texts, Scripture's clearest answer to the question of what happened between Christ's death and resurrection is:

**Christ's soul entered Hades, where it was held under the power of death (ὠδῖνας τοῦ θανάτου, Acts 2:24) until God raised him (2:24). He was in the righteous region of Hades (paradise, Luke 23:43), not in the place of torment.**

Regarding whether he did anything else there—triumphant proclamation to the devil (Lutheranism), releasing the Old Testament saints (Catholicism/Orthodoxy)—Scripture provides several clues but no clear and systematic account. The _Formula of Concord_, Article IX, requiring "no disputation concerning it, but that it should be believed and taught only in the simplest manner," represents an honest theological humility.

Luther himself in the Torgau sermon said:

> "But this man how he may have lain in the grave and yet descended into hell—that, indeed, we shall and must leave unexplained and uncomprehended." (_WA_ 37:62–72)

This humility is not evasion but an acknowledgment of mystery. To maintain humility before mystery, while holding firmly to what the Creed clearly declares—that Christ truly and completely died, and his soul genuinely entered the realm of death—is the final position of this article.

---

## Concluding Summary

Two questions, two levels, two methods:

**The first question**: What does "descended into hell" mean in the Apostles' Creed?

Answer: Historical evidence and the Creed's narrative sequence show that this clause declares Christ's soul genuinely entered the realm of death, occurring after death and before resurrection—the bearing of the complete death of _mot tamut_. The Creed uses this clause to complete Proposition 1 (Christ genuinely bore death), and "rose again on the third day" to declare Proposition 2 (Christ conquered death).

**The second question**: What actually happened between Christ's death and resurrection?

Answer: Acts 2 tells us Christ's soul entered Hades, was held by the power of death, and was then released by God in the resurrection. What he specifically did there, Scripture provides several clues but no systematic answer. The rich readings of the various traditions each have their scriptural grounding, but they are all theological edifices built upon the Creed's concise declaration—not the direct content of what the creedal clause itself announces.

This clause is not a theological footnote. It is the pivot on which the entire drama of redemption turns—from the uttermost depth of _mot tamut_, to the complete victory of life.
