# Descended into Hell: The History and Interpretation of the Apostles' Creed's Most Contested Clause

## I. Introduction

"I believe… in Jesus Christ… who descended into hell, and on the third day rose again from the dead."

These few words in the Apostles' Creed have sparked enduring theological controversy throughout Christian history. Compared to the Creed's other articles, "descended into hell" stands apart: it appeared latest of all, was never formally ratified by any ecumenical council, and remains a point of significant disagreement among the traditions to this day.

The central thesis of this article is that the deepest theological foundation of this clause can be traced back to the death sentence pronounced in Genesis. When God said to Adam, "You shall surely die," the Hebrew original reads **מוֹת תָּמוּת (mot tamut)**—literally "dying you shall die," the most emphatic expression of death available in the Hebrew language, denoting a death that is total and complete: the soul genuinely entering the realm of death itself. The Apostles' Creed's article "descended into hell" is therefore a declaration that Christ bore this death in the full Genesis sense—not merely the cessation of bodily function, but the genuine entry of his soul into the power of death itself.

Two levels of inquiry must be carefully distinguished. The first is: **what does this clause itself mean**—that is, what does it declare within the narrative sequence of the Creed and against its biblical-theological background? The second is: **what did Christ do there**—the question answered by 1 Peter 3:18–20 and related texts. Much of the historical confusion surrounding this clause arises from conflating these two levels. This article focuses on the first level while showing how the two can coexist without contradiction.

## II. Background: The Old Testament Conception of Death

### The Death Sentence in Genesis: Mot Tamut

Any understanding of "descended into hell" must begin with Genesis 2:17. God's warning to Adam in the original Hebrew reads:

**מוֹת תָּמוּת (mot tamut)**

This is the Hebrew infinitive absolute construction, in which a verb is intensified by repetition of its root. The literal translation is "dying you shall die," conveying the sense of "you shall most certainly and completely die." This is no casual warning. It is the most comprehensive declaration of death possible—encompassing the death of the body, the soul's entry into the realm of death, and total severance from God, the source of life.

This declaration forms the starting point of the entire biblical narrative of redemption. Humanity's fundamental predicament is not merely bodily decay, but the soul's imprisonment under the power of death. True redemption, therefore, must squarely address the full weight of mot tamut—the Redeemer must enter this complete death in order to conquer it from within.

It is against this backdrop that "descended into hell" finds its deepest theological meaning. Christ was not merely buried in the ground; his soul truly entered the kind of death declared in Genesis—death as a realm, as a power. This also explains why the Creed requires "descended into hell" after "was buried." Burial speaks to the body; descended into hell speaks to the complete death in the mot tamut sense. Together, they constitute the full bearing of the Genesis death sentence.

### Sheol: The Dwelling of All the Dead

The Hebrew word for the dwelling of the dead is **שְׁאוֹל (Sheol)**—a dark, silent place, cut off from God, where all the dead reside regardless of whether they were righteous or wicked.

The Psalmist describes Sheol thus: "For in death there is no remembrance of you; in Sheol who will give you praise?" (Psalm 6:5). Ecclesiastes states: "Whatever your hand finds to do, do it with your might, for there is no work or thought or knowledge or wisdom in Sheol, to which you are going." (Ecclesiastes 9:10)

The early Old Testament Sheol is a neutral realm of death—not a place of punishment, not a place of reward, but simply the reality of death itself. Yet the Old Testament is not monolithic. An internal tension emerged: on one hand, Sheol is a place of separation from God; on the other, God's sovereignty extends everywhere. Psalm 139:8 declares: "If I ascend to heaven, you are there! If I make my bed in Sheol, you are there!" This tension drove the ongoing development of thought about the afterlife.

### The Second Temple Period: The Internal Distinction within Hades

In the period between the Testaments (roughly 200 BC to AD 100), Jewish theology underwent significant development. The realm of the dead began to have **internal distinctions**: the righteous and the wicked occupy different regions while awaiting the final judgment.

Luke 16's contrast between "Abraham's bosom" and the place of torment reflects a conception already mature by this period. Crucially, both Lazarus and the rich man are **within Hades** (the realm of the dead)—they simply occupy different sides of it, separated by a great chasm. The righteous dwell in the region of paradise (Abraham's bosom); the unrighteous in the region of torment.

This internal distinction is essential for understanding Luke 23:43. When Jesus said to the thief on the cross, "Today you will be with me in paradise," the "paradise" here is not heaven itself but the region of the righteous within Hades. Christ's soul entered Hades at death, but on the side where the righteous dwell. This coheres perfectly with Acts 2:27, where Peter cites Psalm 16:10—"you will not abandon my soul to Hades"—as a prophecy of the resurrection: the soul **did enter** Hades, and was then raised out of it. The Lutheran argument that Luke 23:43 proves Christ's soul went immediately to heaven (and therefore the descent followed the resurrection) depends on equating "paradise" with "heaven"—an equation that the vocabulary of the period does not require.

## III. The Creed's Narrative Sequence: The Strongest Internal Evidence

In all the debates surrounding this clause, the Apostles' Creed's own narrative sequence is the most important and most frequently overlooked internal evidence:

> Suffered → Died → **Was buried** → **Descended into hell** → Rose again on the third day → Ascended into heaven

This sequence is itself a powerful theological statement.

**"Descended into hell" is plainly before the resurrection.** This directly determines several things.

First, it describes what happened **after death and before resurrection**—not something occurring on the cross, and not something occurring after the resurrection.

Second, it stands alongside "was buried" but **does not repeat it**. Burial speaks of the body being placed in the tomb. Descended into hell speaks of the soul entering the realm of death. Together they bear the full weight of mot tamut's two-sided reality.

Third, **Calvin's reading contravenes this sequence**. If "descended into hell" refers to Christ bearing God's judgment on the cross, it ought to appear before or alongside "died," not after "was buried." Scaer makes this point with precision: the word order places the descent after the burial, not after the death. For Calvin's reading to work, the Creed's narrative must be disordered.

Fourth, **the Lutheran reading also creates tension with the sequence**. Lutheranism places the descent after the vivification (ζῳοποιηθείς)—after the resurrection—as the first act of Christ's state of exaltation. But the Creed plainly places the descent **before** the resurrection. This requires additional explanation to reconcile with the creedal formula as it stands.

Taking the sequence at face value, the most natural reading is that "descended into hell" marks **the completion of Christ's state of humiliation**—an event after death and before resurrection, in which the soul genuinely entered the realm of the dead.

## IV. New Testament Support

### Acts 2:27, 31: The Most Direct Evidence

In his Pentecost sermon, Peter cites Psalm 16:10:

> "For you will not abandon my soul to Hades, or let your Holy One see corruption."

Peter's argument is that David prophesied the resurrection of Christ by saying his soul would **not be left in Hades**—which presupposes that Christ's soul **did enter Hades**, and was then raised from it. This is the clearest New Testament statement of the complete narrative: soul enters Hades, soul is raised out of Hades. It aligns perfectly with the Creed's sequence.

### Ephesians 4:9: The Contrast of Descent and Ascent

> "In saying, 'He ascended,' what does it mean but that he had also descended into the lower regions of the earth?"

The descending language of entering hell is contrasted with the ascending language of going to the Father's right hand in the ascension. By Christ's descending into hell and ascending into heaven, an immediately recognizable contrast is established. This descent-ascent arc matches precisely the Creed's movement and the mot tamut framework's emphasis: down to the deepest point, up to the highest.

### Romans 10:7

> "Who will descend into the abyss? (that is, to bring Christ up from the dead)"

Here "descending into the abyss" is directly linked to Christ's resurrection—the abyss is where he truly was, and the resurrection is the act of coming up from it.

### Luke 23:43: Paradise Within Hades

> "Truly, I say to you, today you will be with me in paradise."

As discussed above, in the Second Temple framework, "paradise" designates the region of the righteous within Hades, not heaven itself. This verse does not contradict Christ's entry into the realm of death; rather, it specifies which region of that realm he occupied—the side of the righteous. The resurrection is then the act of coming out of Hades altogether—which is precisely what Acts 2:27 says happened.

## V. The Traditions and Their Assessment

### A Note on Scope

The following assessment focuses on a specific question: **what does the Apostles' Creed's clause "descended into hell" itself declare**—not the exegesis of 1 Peter 3:18–20. The Lutheran reading of 1 Peter as a triumphant proclamation to the spirits answers the question of what Christ **did** there. The creedal clause answers the prior question of **when this event occurred and why it matters**. These are two different levels of inquiry that can coexist without contradiction.

### Eastern Orthodoxy: The Descent and the Conquest of Death

The Eastern Orthodox reading of this clause is the most elaborate and the most dramatic. Christ's descent into Hades (Greek: **Κατάβασις εἰς ᾅδου**) is a central theme of Orthodox Paschal theology. In the Orthodox understanding, Christ actively entered the power of death in order to shatter it from within. This theme finds its most vivid visual expression in the Orthodox Paschal icon (the **Anastasis**): Christ stands atop the broken gates of hell, grasps Adam and Eve by the wrists and raises them, while Satan lies trampled beneath his feet.

Of all the traditions, Orthodoxy is most naturally aligned with the reading proposed here: Hades is Hades, Christ truly entered the realm of death, and the resurrection is the act of coming out of it in triumph. This coheres with the Creed's word order and with the mot tamut framework.

### Roman Catholicism: The Limbus Patrum

Medieval Catholic theology developed a more elaborate taxonomy of the underworld, distinguishing the limbus patrum (where the Old Testament righteous awaited redemption) from purgatory and hell proper. Christ descended to release the patriarchs and bring them into heaven. This reading accepts the premise that Christ genuinely entered the realm of the dead, and is broadly consistent with the framework proposed here. The disagreement concerns the scope and purpose of the descent rather than its basic character.

### Lutheranism: The Victor's Triumphant Proclamation

Lutheranism holds that Christ descended as conqueror to proclaim his victory to the devil and evil spirits, placing this event after the vivification—after the resurrection—as the first act of Christ's state of exaltation.

The Lutheran exegesis of **1 Peter 3:18–20** is persuasive on its own terms: ἐκήρυξεν (proclamation) does carry the force of an authoritative announcement, and Colossians 2:15 supports the triumphal theme. But placing the descent after the resurrection creates tension with **the Creed's own sequence**, which puts the descent before the resurrection. The Lutheran reading requires the creedal order to be either reinterpreted or explained away. This is a cost the framework proposed here does not incur.

### Reformed (Calvin): The Torments of Hell on the Cross

Calvin interpreted "descended into hell" as Christ's bearing of God's complete judgment and forsakenness on the cross—the essence of hell's torment experienced in the soul. Calvin's theological concern is genuine: complete atonement does involve the soul's extreme anguish, and "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" (Mark 15:34) is powerful biblical evidence for this dimension of Christ's suffering.

However, Calvin's reading faces two difficulties that are difficult to overcome.

The first is **the word-order problem**. If "descended into hell" refers to suffering on the cross, it ought to appear before or alongside "died," not after "was buried." The Creed's sequence directly undermines Calvin's interpretation—a point Scaer makes explicitly.

The second is **the redundancy problem**. If the descent only recapitulates the cross-suffering already expressed in "suffered," it adds no independent theological content and the clause becomes superfluous.

The proper relationship between Calvin and the framework proposed here is this: Calvin's theological instinct is correct—this clause concerns a dimension of death deeper than mere physical burial. But he locates it at the wrong moment and substitutes an inner experience for what mot tamut requires: genuine entry into the realm of death.

## VI. Synthesis

### The Natural Meaning of the Clause

Drawing together the Creed's narrative sequence, the original meaning of Hades, the mot tamut framework, and the cumulative weight of the New Testament evidence, the most natural reading is:

> **After his death, Christ's soul genuinely entered the realm of the dead (Hades). This occurred after the burial and before the resurrection. Burial demonstrates that the body truly died; descended into hell demonstrates that the soul truly entered the domain of death's power. The resurrection is the act of coming out of that deepest place—the genuine conquest of death in its fullness.**

This reading:

- Follows the Creed's narrative sequence without remainder
- Requires no symbolic reinterpretation of Hades
- Coheres with the argument of Acts 2:27
- Gives "descended into hell" theological content independent of "was buried"
- Gives "rose again on the third day" its full theological weight

### What the Traditions Contribute

The detailed readings of each tradition—liberating the Old Testament saints, proclaiming victory over the devil, bearing the divine wrath—are theological edifices built upon the Creed's bare statement. They answer the question of what Christ did there. The Creed itself is silent on this question. That silence is not a denial; these readings each have genuine scriptural grounding. But they are theological developments beyond the creedal clause itself, and should not be read back into it as if they were what the clause directly declares.

### The Deepest Foundation Against Docetism

Historically, this clause was most likely added to the Creed to counter Docetism—the heresy that denied Christ had a real body and therefore only appeared to suffer and die. The mot tamut framework gives this apologetic concern a far deeper scriptural foundation than the simple affirmation "he really died." Christ did not merely appear to die; he bore the complete death declared in Genesis—his soul truly entered the realm where the dead dwell. This is the most thorough refutation of Docetism possible.

---

## VII. Conclusion

"Descended into hell" is the article in the Apostles' Creed with the shortest history, the most controversy, and the greatest divergence of interpretation. It entered the Creed in the fourth century through gradual liturgical practice, never receiving authoritative interpretation from any ecumenical council.

Historically, each tradition has emphasized a different aspect: Orthodoxy sees the cosmic drama of Christ conquering death; Catholicism sees the historical completion of redemption extending to the Old Testament righteous; Lutheranism sees the conqueror's victorious proclamation to the powers of darkness; Reformed theology sees the deepest dimension of the cross's atoning work. These divergences reflect deeper differences in soteriology, Christology, and eschatology. But they are answers to a question the Creed itself does not ask—what Christ did there. The Creed simply declares that he went.

If we ask what the clause itself declares, within its own narrative logic, the answer is given by Genesis and confirmed by the Creed's sequence: Christ bore the complete death of mot tamut. His suffering and death addressed the bodily dimension; "descended into hell" addresses the dimension of the soul—the soul genuinely entered the domain ruled by the power of death. "Rose again on the third day" is then the act of coming out of that uttermost depth.

The Creed's narrative arc is therefore: **descent to the lowest point, ascent to the highest**. "Descended into hell" is the nadir of that arc—the precondition for resurrection to be a genuine triumph over death in its fullness, and the indispensable pivot on which the entire drama of redemption turns.
