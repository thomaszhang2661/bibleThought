# 好人是不是永远打不过坏人

## ——兼谈基督徒如何面对邪恶的权柄

小时候读金庸，我一直有个想不通的问题：好人是不是永远打不过坏人？

因为坏人不按规矩来。他会骗，会偷袭，会下毒，会挟持人质。而好人呢，还要按"君子"的标准约束自己——不能趁人之危，不能背后下手，输了还得拱手说一声承让。那这世界岂不是注定要被坏人统治？君子得比坏人高多少个段位，才能既守着规矩、又赢下这场仗？做个好人，也太难了吧。

后来看周星驰的《功夫》，结尾那记从天而降的如来神掌，强大的武力压制也没能一掌把"火云邪神"打服。真正让火云邪神降服的，是星爷那句"想学？我教你啊"——不记仇，还为你好。这股邪门的豁达，把"火云邪神"降服了。

这个夏天正好是美国建国二百五十周年，我们也开始重新思考一个问题：当年美国独立战争，说到底是为了不给英国政府纳税，这算不算违背了罗马书十三章"顺服掌权者"的教导？

顺着这个问题往下想，我有很多困惑，跟周围的牧师、教授讨论后整理一下自己的思路，供大家参考。

## 一、我们的第一反应，是"我得做点什么"

面对在上的、尤其是作恶的权柄，我们几乎是本能地想：我得做点什么，抵挡它，抵抗它。

这里要先岔开讲一对概念：**左手的权柄和右手的权柄。**

所谓左手权柄，就是这个世界上的权柄——地位、影响力、金钱、上帝借着各种身份交在我们手里的那点权力。比如对孩子，你想让他养成或者戒掉某个习惯，左手的办法就是胡萝卜加大棒：你不如何如何，我就如何如何。见效快。而右手的权柄，是为孩子祷告，为自己祷告，求主让孩子在你身上看见上帝的爱，于是他看到父母对他的爱，也发自心底的爱父母，甘心去行那看为美的事。

要说清楚的是：**这两只手，都是上帝的手，都是好的，各有各的位置。** 掌刀剑的官长尽本分，也是在做上帝左手的工。所以我们的毛病，从来不是"用了左手"，而是常常伸错了手——该跪下祷告的时候，我们抄起了刀；该老老实实尽本分的时候，我们又只会空谈爱与和平。

那么，面对在我们之上的、甚至是邪恶的权柄，我们能不能理直气壮地抄起左手，去不顺服、去抵抗？

## 二、耶稣，总是"不愿"用左手

当我翻圣经，看到的是这样一幅画面：耶稣似乎总是**reluctant**，总是不情愿动用他的左手权柄。

他被抓的那一夜，彼得抽刀削掉了大祭司仆人的耳朵。耶稣却说，你以为我不能求我父现在就派十二营天使来吗（太26:53）？他当然有这个权柄，大到无法想象，但他选择不用。

旧约里的耶稣的预表约瑟也是。无缘无故被弟兄扔进坑里，卖作奴隶，被诬陷下监，被酒政忘得干干净净。整个过程里，你几乎找不到约瑟"自救"的记载，找不到他怎样施展左手权柄去扳回局面。但上帝从死亡和粪堆中提拔穷乏人，就像天父后来从埃及召出他的儿子，就像天父从死亡中招出他的爱子。

出埃及的故事也是类似。上帝没有呼召以色列人自己揭竿而起，他亲自做他们的战士和盾牌。"耶和华必为你们争战，你们只管静默，不要作声"（出14:14）。

## 三、一个陷阱：我们容易用"爱邻舍"给不顺服背书

当我们觉得非做点什么不可的时候，手边最顺的一个理由，往往是"爱邻舍"。为了社会，为了公义，为了大多数人的好处，我得站出来对抗这不义的权柄。

但这里藏着两个坑。

**第一，哦我们所要抵抗的那个作恶的掌权者，他本身也是你的邻舍。** 耶稣讲好撒玛利亚人的时候，那个反问"谁是我的邻舍"，答案里没有任何范围的划线——上帝要我们爱周围所有的人，也包括那个行不义的在上者。这一点圣经说得毫不含糊：有人强逼你走一里路，你就同他走二里；有人拿你的里衣，连外衣也由他拿去；有人打你左脸，连右脸也转过来由他打。

**第二，很多邪恶的权柄，恰恰是打着"爱邻舍"的旗号来的。** 为了社会公义，为了多数人的好处，为了一个伟大、光荣、正确的明天——当一种"真理"配上一种"博爱"，膨胀成不容置疑的宏大叙事，还有谁拦得住它的权柄？"英特纳雄耐尔就一定要实现"，就等着我们这一代人去把它实现——闵采尔当年就是这么想的，他也确实动了手。历史反复证明，最凶的暴政，往往不是以"我要作恶"的名义登场，而是以"我要救你"的名义。有一个教授是这样调侃的：“老子就是要为人民服务，什么叫问问你愿不愿被服务，你不要不识抬举”。这样的权柄和霸气是从哪里来的，历史上很多次，恰恰是从一些很宏大的好的愿景中来的。


## 四、因为，上帝并没有缺席

我们之所以急，很多时候是因为心里冒出一种社会责任感和紧迫感，觉得上帝好像缺席了，撒手不管了，得靠我们自己顶上去。

这让我想起《功夫》里那个露着半个屁股、想试试自己是不是绝世高手的“酱爆”，被包租婆一巴掌拍翻在地——"看来，他不是。"我们急着救世界，很多时候底气来自一个悄悄的信心对象的漂移：从信靠上帝转移到信靠自己。我自己成了我生命的主角。

但主角从来不是我，也不是缺席了。上帝没有缺席，他在，他一直都在。他造了这世界，也小心地护理着这个世界。他知道的比我多，他比我良善，他比我有能力——所以我可以安心睡觉。真到了危险临头，若有必要，他也不吝惜使用神迹来搭救我。

雅各当年用尽人的心机去骗那个"祝福",结果把自己弄到一无所有、连夜逃命,躲哥哥的追杀。可就在旷野那一夜,他梦见一道梯子接通天地,天使在上头上去下来,耶和华站在梯子以上,向这个刚骗完人、正在逃亡的骗子,主动应许看顾他、祝福他——不是因为他配,恰恰在他最不配的时候。而这道梯子,耶稣说,就是他自己(约1:51):天与地断掉的那条线,在基督身上重新接上了。上帝借着耶稣,亲自下到我们中间,来看顾我们、祝福我们。他也曾在梦中叫醒耶稣肉身的父亲约瑟，吩咐他带着马利亚和孩子连夜逃往埃及。他让钉死的耶稣从死里复活。他也不吝惜差天使把彼得从监里领出来，继续去传福音。那记"比地上一切阴招不知道高到哪里去了"的如来神掌，其实一直都在。

所以保罗才说，你要**思想神的恩慈和严厉**（罗11:22）：严厉临到那跌倒的，恩慈临到你。请注意这个分工——**审判作恶者，是上帝自己的职分（他的"严厉"）；正因为这活儿归他，我们才不必抢过来，替他动手。** 约伯回望自己颠沛的一生，能这样说："你将生命和慈爱赐给我，你也眷顾保全我的心灵"（伯10:12）。你我大概也能这样说。

## 五、那么，我们到底能做什么？

面对邪恶权柄，基督徒的**默认姿态**是忍耐、信靠、不以牙还牙。但"默认不还手"不等于"什么都不做"。至少有下面四件事，是我们可以做、也应当做的。

**一、我们可以向上帝呼求，甚至哀求——甚至是咒诅诗那样的哀求。**

诗篇里有一批"咒诅诗"，今天几乎被我们悄悄跳过去了。坐在软乎乎的沙发上，确实很难开口祷告"神啊，求你折断他们口中的牙齿"。但你若仔细读就会发现一件要紧的事：**诗人从头到尾，自己不动手。** 他不去报仇，他把仇交在神手里——"伸冤在我，我必报应"（申32:35）。咒诅诗不是抄刀，恰恰相反，它是把那股"我真想抄刀"的冲动，原封不动地交托给上帝的右手方式。这也是为什么它接得住我们心里最黑的那点东西，又不至于让我们真的堕入以恶报恶。

而且诗人祷告是有次序的：他先求仇敌回转——"求你使他们满面含羞，使他们寻求你耶和华的名"（诗83:16），实在执迷不悟了，才求审判。这不就是《功夫》结尾那句"想学？我教你啊"吗？

这不是软弱，也不是逃避。祷告之前，诗人先省察自己，知道自己并不比对方更配得上帝的惩罚；祷告之中，他把那股冲动一寸不留地交出去。求主伸冤。

**二、我们可以逃跑。**

对，逃跑。听起来好像很没出息，其实是圣经允许、甚至吩咐的。耶稣自己说："有人在这城里逼迫你们，就逃到那城里去"（太10:23），他也一次次地"退去"。保罗被人围堵，是被门徒装在筐子里从城墙上缒下去的（徒9:25）。约瑟半夜被叫醒，带着孩子逃往埃及。大卫逃扫罗、逃押沙龙，逃了大半辈子。逃跑不是没信心，很多时候，逃跑才是那个让开位置、把舞台交还给上帝的信心动作。

但逃跑有它的边界：好牧人不撇下羊群自己跑，撇下羊群的是雇工（约10:12-13）。所以逃与守的分寸，全看你的**呼召**——你是可以带家人离开的那个人，还是被托付了不能丢下的那群羊。朋霍费尔1939年逃到了美国，安全了，却又主动买船票回去，因为他认定自己的呼召是与本国的人同担命运。逃或不逃，在自己所承担的责任里。

**三、我们可以把枪口抬高一寸。**

那句被反复引用的话是这么说的：作为一个心智健全的人，你本可以把枪口抬高一寸。意思是，当你被逼着去执行恶，哪怕没法当场拒绝，你仍然可以选择消极地执行、悄悄地留一手。

圣经里满是这样的人。法老下令杀光希伯来男婴，接生婆施弗拉和普阿"因为敬畏神"，就是不照办，还编了套说辞糊弄过去——结果神反倒厚待她们，为她们成立家室（出1:15-21）。俄巴底在亚哈的宫里当差，却偷偷把一百个先知藏进山洞，供他们饼和水（王上18）。东方博士被希律当成线人，却索性绕道回国，不去报信（太2:12）。这些都是在**自己的职分之内**，对邪恶命令的消极不服从。它的底层逻辑，就是彼得那句"顺从神，不顺从人，是应当的"（徒5:29）。

**四、我们可以在职分之内，合法地动用左手权柄。**

这是最需要分寸的一条。一五五〇年，马德堡城的牧师们写下一份认信，为他们抵抗帝国政府辩护，讲的正是"下级执政官"的道理：权位低于皇帝的各级官长，也有治理的呼召，这呼召给了他们介入、抵制上级的路径。但他们的抵抗窄得很——**只在极端处境下、只为捍卫福音、作为最后手段、而且极不情愿地**提出来。他们甚至担心这道理被坏人拿去当遮羞布，被好人在一时血气里滥用。他们说得很清楚：能顺服时，我们乐意顺服。

用到今天：作为一个公民，本身就是一种呼召，它给了你一份实实在在的左手权柄——抗议、请愿、投票。这些都可以正当地用来抵制不义。前提是，你守在自己的呼召之内，别越位，不把"公民"膨胀成"救世主"。在尊重和顺服的态度中行使左手的权柄。

把这四条摆在一起，我们不难发现这些都极容易被滥用。所以每次动念之前，我们先问自己一句：我是真的被逼着犯罪、福音真的受了攻击，还是仅仅——我自己不爽？马德堡牧师们抵抗的门槛，就卡在这个区别上。

## 六、回到那场独立战争

那么，二百五十年前那场为了税收而起的独立战争，到底是不是犯罪？

用马德堡的尺子量一量，它落在一个尴尬的中间地带。它确实是由下级执政官（各殖民地议会）领头的——这一条对得上。但它所争的，是税负与代表权，而不是为了捍卫福音；它面对的也算不上什么大恶——跟今天集权政府内部发生的那些事相比，当年的英国政府，说实话，还算相当克制。所以我们被办法替它封圣——按罗马书十三章的尺子，它就是不顺服，明明白白。

对神来说，把帝国邦国翻来覆去不过是件小事——路德说得直白：他有时把一个国赐给恶棍，从好人手里夺走，藉着背信之人的诡诈，或单单藉着世袭，就悄然成就。神的护理向来不挑手段：他能借着雅各的欺骗赐福，能借着约瑟弟兄的恶意赐福，同样能借着一场违背了罗马书十三章顺服之命的独立战争赐福。所以我们尽可以为他借此赐下的一切——信仰自由、法治、权利法案——向他感恩；感恩是安全的。危险的是另一件事：把这场本就站不住"义战"之名的战争，反过来镀上一层神圣的光环，当作不容置疑的救赎义举来纪念。感恩祝福，与神圣化战争本身，是两回事。罪仍旧是罪；神从中带出善来，并不为任何人开脱。

好人是不是永远打不过坏人？在人的领域，照着人的能力，应该说这是对的。但那记贯穿死亡的如来神掌一直都在，从没收回去过。保卫世界和平这么重的担子，其实可以放心交给祂扛。我们要做的，是在他扛着的时候，别乱伸手，守好自己那一小方职分，该祷告祷告，该逃就逃，该把枪口抬高一寸就抬高一寸。

到了那日，义人诚然有善报；在地上果然有施行审判的神（诗58:11）。

# Can Good Guys Never Beat the Bad Guys?

## —And How Christians Should Face Evil Authority

When I was a kid reading Jin Yong (the most beloved writer of _wuxia_—the sweeping Chinese martial-arts epics), one question always stumped me: can good guys never beat bad guys?

Because the bad guys don't play by the rules. They cheat, they ambush, they poison, they take hostages. The good guy, meanwhile, still has to hold himself to the standard of the _junzi_—the Confucian gentleman: no kicking a man when he's down, no striking from behind, and when he loses, he still has to cup his hands and murmur something gracious. Doesn't that mean the world is destined to be ruled by the bad guys? How many levels above his enemy would a gentleman have to be, to win the fight _and_ keep the rules? Being a good guy is just too hard, isn't it?

Later I watched Stephen Chow's slapstick martial-arts film _Kung Fu Hustle_. At the climax, even the Buddha Palm—the legendary ultimate technique that descends out of the sky—couldn't pound the Beast (火云邪神, the film's supreme villain, literally "Fire-Cloud Evil God") into submission by sheer force. What actually brought the Beast to heel was the hero's one line: "Want to learn it? I'll teach you." No grudge held, only goodwill. It was that strange, oversized magnanimity that finally subdued him.

This summer happens to be the 250th anniversary of American independence, so we've found ourselves reopening an old question: the War of Independence was, at bottom, a refusal to pay taxes to the British crown—did that violate Romans 13 and its command to "submit to the governing authorities"?

Following that question down the rabbit hole left me with a lot of confusion. After talking it through with the pastors and professors around me, here's my attempt to sort out my own thinking, offered for whatever it's worth.

## I. Our first instinct is: "I have to _do_ something."

Faced with an authority over us—especially an authority doing evil—our almost instinctive reaction is: I have to do something. Resist it. Fight it.

Let me first step aside and introduce a pair of concepts: **left-hand authority and right-hand authority.**

Left-hand authority is power in this world—status, influence, money, whatever bit of leverage God has placed in our hands through our various roles. Take a child: if you want him to pick up or drop a habit, the left-handed method is carrot and stick—if you don't do such-and-such, then I'll do such-and-such. It works fast. Right-hand authority, by contrast, is to pray for your child and for yourself, asking the Lord to let him see God's love in you—so that, having seen your love for him, he loves you back from the heart, and willingly does what is good.

To be clear: **both hands are God's hands, both are good, each has its proper place.** The magistrate who bears the sword and does his duty is also doing God's left-handed work. So our problem is never that we "used the left hand." It's that we keep reaching for the wrong hand—snatching up the sword when we should be on our knees praying, and mouthing empty platitudes about love and peace when we should be quietly doing our duty.

So then: faced with an authority above us—even an evil one—may we in good conscience grab the left hand and disobey, and resist?

## II. Jesus is always "unwilling" to use the left hand.

When I turn to Scripture, the picture I find is this: Jesus seems always **reluctant**, always unwilling, to deploy his left-hand power.

On the night he was arrested, Peter drew a sword and sliced off the ear of the high priest's servant. But Jesus said: Do you think I cannot call on my Father, and he will at once put more than twelve legions of angels at my disposal (Matt. 26:53)? He certainly had that authority—unimaginably vast—and he chose not to use it.

Joseph, the Old Testament type of Christ, is the same. Thrown into a pit by his brothers for no reason, sold into slavery, framed and jailed, forgotten by the cupbearer. Through the whole ordeal you can barely find a record of Joseph "saving himself," of him working some left-handed lever to turn the situation around. Yet God lifts the needy from death and the dunghill—just as the Father would later call his Son out of Egypt, just as the Father summoned his beloved Son out of death.

The Exodus runs the same way. God did not call Israel to rise up and revolt; he himself became their warrior and their shield. "The LORD will fight for you; you need only to be still" (Ex. 14:14).

## III. A trap: we're quick to justify disobedience with "love your neighbor."

When we feel we simply must do something, the handiest justification is usually "love your neighbor." For society, for justice, for the good of the majority, I have to stand up against this unjust power.

But there are two pits hidden here.

**First, the very authority you're resisting—the one doing evil—is also your neighbor.** When Jesus told the parable of the Good Samaritan, that counter-question "Who is my neighbor?" drew no line around any category. God wants us to love everyone around us, including the unjust one set over us. On this, Scripture is unambiguous: if someone forces you to go one mile, go two; if someone takes your shirt, hand over your coat as well; if someone strikes your left cheek, turn the right to him too.

**Second, a great deal of evil authority comes precisely wrapped in the banner of "love your neighbor."** For social justice, for the good of the many, for a great, glorious, and correct tomorrow—when a "truth" is paired with a "universal love" and swells into an unquestionable grand narrative, who is left with the power to check it? "The Internationale shall surely be realized"—and it just waits for our generation to go realize it. Thomas Müntzer thought exactly this way, and he really did take up arms. History proves it over and over: the fiercest tyrannies rarely arrive under the banner of "I want to do evil." They arrive under the banner of "I want to save you." One Chinese Politics Professor put it with grim humor: "I _will_ serve the people, —what do you mean, ask whether you _want_ to be served? You'd better know what's good for you. Where does that kind of power and swagger come from? Again and again in history, it comes straight out of some grand and beautiful vision.

## IV. Because God has not gone absent.

Part of why we panic is that a sense of social duty and urgency wells up inside us, and we feel as if God has gone absent—thrown up his hands, stopped caring—so it's on us to step in.

It reminds me of the character in _Kung Fu Hustle_—Jiang Bao, the one with half his backside hanging out, itching to test whether he might secretly be a kung-fu master—who gets flattened by a single slap from the Landlady: "Guess not." When we rush to go save the world, our nerve often rests on a quiet drift in the object of our faith: from trusting God to trusting ourselves. I've made myself the protagonist of my own life.

But the protagonist was never me—and he never went absent. God has not gone absent. He is here; he has always been here. He made this world and tends it with care. He knows more than I do, he is more good than I am, he is more able than I am—so I can go to sleep in peace. And if real danger comes, should it be needed, he does not begrudge a miracle to rescue me.

Jacob once exhausted every human trick to steal that "blessing," and ended up with nothing, fleeing for his life by night, running from a brother out for his blood. Yet that very night in the wilderness he dreamed of a ladder joining heaven and earth, angels ascending and descending on it, and the LORD standing above it—promising this fresh-off-a-swindle fugitive that he would watch over him and bless him. Not because he deserved it; precisely when he deserved it least. And that ladder, Jesus says, is himself (John 1:51): the severed line between heaven and earth, reconnected in Christ. God, through Jesus, comes down into our midst himself, to watch over us and bless us. He is the one who woke Joseph, Jesus' earthly father, in a dream and told him to take Mary and the child and flee to Egypt by night. He is the one who raised the crucified Jesus from the dead. He is the one who did not begrudge sending an angel to lead Peter out of prison to go on preaching the gospel. That "Buddha Palm"—the one that towers immeasurably above every dirty trick on earth—has been there the whole time.

That's why Paul says: **consider the kindness and the sternness of God** (Rom. 11:22)—sternness to those who fell, kindness to you. Notice the division of labor: **judging the evildoer is God's own office (his "sternness"); and precisely because that job is his, we don't have to snatch it away and do it for him.** Job, looking back over his own battered life, could say: "You gave me life and showed me kindness, and in your providence you watched over my spirit" (Job 10:12). You and I can probably say the same.

## V. So what _can_ we do?

Faced with evil authority, the Christian's **default posture** is patience, trust, and no returning evil for evil. But "not hitting back by default" is not the same as "doing nothing." There are at least four things we can—and should—do.

**1. We can cry out to God, even plead with him—even the kind of pleading you find in the imprecatory psalms.**

There's a set of "cursing psalms" in the Psalter that we quietly skip over these days. Sunk into a soft sofa, it's hard to open your mouth and pray, "O God, break the teeth in their mouths." But read them closely and you'll notice something crucial: **from beginning to end, the psalmist never lifts a hand himself.** He does not take revenge; he hands the vengeance to God—"It is mine to avenge; I will repay" (Deut. 32:35). The imprecatory psalm is not a drawn sword. It's the very opposite: it's the right-handed way of handing that "I really want to draw a sword" impulse over to God, whole and unspent. Which is exactly why it can hold the blackest thing in our hearts and still keep us from actually sinking into evil-for-evil.

And the psalmist prays in a certain order: first he asks for the enemy's turning—"Cover their faces with shame, LORD, so that they will seek your name" (Ps. 83:16)—and only if they remain hardened does he ask for judgment. Isn't this just the hero's line at the end of _Kung Fu Hustle_: "Want to learn it? I'll teach you"?

This is not weakness, and it is not evasion. Before he prays, the psalmist searches himself, knowing he is no more deserving of God's punishment than his enemy is; and in the praying, he hands over that impulse without keeping back an inch. Lord, avenge.

**2. We can flee.**

Yes, flee. It sounds gutless, but it's something Scripture permits—even commands. Jesus himself said, "When you are persecuted in one place, flee to another" (Matt. 10:23), and he "withdrew" again and again. When Paul was surrounded, the disciples lowered him over the city wall in a basket (Acts 9:25). Joseph was woken in the night and fled with the child to Egypt. David fled Saul, fled Absalom—spent the better part of his life running. Fleeing is not a lack of faith; very often, fleeing is precisely the act of faith that steps aside and hands the stage back to God.

But fleeing has a boundary: the good shepherd does not abandon the flock and run—the one who runs is the hired hand (John 10:12–13). So the line between fleeing and standing depends entirely on your **calling**—whether you're the one free to take his family and go, or the one entrusted with a flock he cannot leave behind. Bonhoeffer fled to America in 1939, safe—and then bought a ticket back, because he judged his calling to be sharing the fate of his own people. To flee or to stay: it lies within the responsibility you've been given.

**3. We can raise the muzzle by an inch.**

There's an oft-quoted line—supposedly from the trial of a Berlin Wall border guard—that runs: as a person of sound mind, you could have raised your muzzle by an inch. The point being that when you're forced to carry out an evil order, even if you can't refuse outright on the spot, you can still choose to execute it half-heartedly, to quietly hold something back.

Scripture is full of such people. Pharaoh ordered every Hebrew boy killed, but the midwives Shiphrah and Puah, "because they feared God," simply didn't do it—and cooked up a story to fob him off—and God dealt well with them and gave them families of their own (Ex. 1:15–21). Obadiah served in Ahab's court, yet secretly hid a hundred prophets in caves and supplied them with bread and water (1 Kings 18). The Magi, treated by Herod as informants, simply took another road home and never reported back (Matt. 2:12). These are all cases of passive noncompliance with an evil command, from **within one's own station**. The bedrock logic is Peter's line: "We must obey God rather than men" (Acts 5:29).

**4. We can, within our station, lawfully wield left-hand authority.**

This is the one that most demands a sense of proportion. In 1550 the pastors of the city of Magdeburg wrote a confession defending their resistance to the imperial government, and its core is precisely the doctrine of the "lesser magistrate": rulers ranked below the emperor also have a calling to govern, and that calling gives them a path to intervene against and resist a superior authority. But the resistance they defend is _narrow_—advanced **only in extreme circumstances, only to defend the gospel, only as a last resort, and only with great reluctance.** They even worried the doctrine would be seized by wicked men as a fig leaf, or abused by good men in a flush of hot blood. They put it plainly: when we can submit, we submit gladly.

Applied to today: being a citizen is itself a kind of calling, and it hands you a real piece of left-hand authority—protest, petition, the vote. These can all be legitimately used to resist injustice. The condition is that you stay within your calling, don't overstep, and don't inflate "citizen" into "savior." Wield the left hand in an attitude of respect and submission.

Set these four side by side and it's not hard to see that every one of them is easily abused. So before we act on any of them, we first ask ourselves one question: am I really being forced to sin, is the gospel really under attack—or am I just—annoyed? The Magdeburg pastors' threshold for resistance turns on exactly that distinction.

## VI. Back to that War of Independence.

So: that 250-year-old war of independence, fought over taxes—was it a crime?

Measured by the Magdeburg yardstick, it lands in an awkward middle zone. It was indeed led by **lesser magistrates** (the colonial assemblies)—that box gets checked. But what it contended for was tax burdens and representation, **not** the defense of the gospel; nor was it facing any great evil—set beside what goes on inside today's authoritarian regimes, the British government of the day was, frankly, rather restrained. So we have no business canonizing it—by the measure of Romans 13, it was disobedience, plainly.

For God, toppling empires and principalities is a trifling thing. Luther put it bluntly: now and then he hands a kingdom to a scoundrel and takes one from a good man—sometimes through the treachery of faithless men, sometimes by nothing more than inheritance—and thinks nothing of it. God's providence has never been fussy about its instruments: he can bless through Jacob's deceit, bless through the malice of Joseph's brothers, and just as well bless through a war of independence that broke the submission commanded in Romans 13. So we may indeed give him thanks for everything he has granted us through it—religious freedom, the rule of law, the Bill of Rights; the thanksgiving is safe. The danger lies elsewhere: in taking a war that never earned the name "just" and draping it, in reverse, in a halo of the sacred—commemorating it as an unquestionable, redemptive act of righteousness. To give thanks for a blessing, and to sanctify the war itself, are two different things. The sin remains sin; that God brought good out of it excuses no one.

Can good guys never beat bad guys? In the human arena, by human strength, the answer is probably yes—they can't. But that Buddha Palm that passes clean through death has been there the whole time, and was never withdrawn. The heavy work of keeping the world's peace can be safely handed to _him_ to carry. Our part, while he carries it, is to not go flailing our own arms—to guard well our own small patch of station: pray when it's time to pray, flee when it's time to flee, and raise the muzzle an inch when it's time to raise the muzzle an inch.

On that day, "surely the righteous still are rewarded; surely there is a God who judges the earth" (Ps. 58:11).
